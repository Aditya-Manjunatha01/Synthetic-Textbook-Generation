To understand the practical utility of the Gibbs free energy, we consider its application to chemical thermodynamics. In a laboratory or industrial setting, most reactions occur at constant temperature $T$ and constant pressure $P$ (typically atmospheric pressure). Under these constraints, the change in Gibbs free energy, $\Delta G$, serves as the definitive criterion for spontaneity: a process is spontaneous if $\Delta G < 0$, at equilibrium if $\Delta G = 0$, and non-spontaneous in the forward direction if $\Delta G > 0$.

For a chemical reaction, the change in Gibbs free energy is related to the change in enthalpy $\Delta H$ and the change in entropy $\Delta S$ by the fundamental relation:

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$$

where $\Delta H$ represents the heat exchanged at constant pressure and $\Delta S$ represents the change in the system's disorder. This equation reveals a fundamental competition between the energetic drive toward lower enthalpy (exothermicity) and the statistical drive toward higher entropy.

### Worked Example: The Synthesis of Ammonia

Consider the Haber-Bosch process, the industrial synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen gases:

$$\text{N}_2(g) + 3\text{H}_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2\text{NH}_3(g)$$

To determine if this reaction is spontaneous at a given temperature, we use standard state values (denoted by the superscript $\circ$), which refer to substances in their most stable form at 1 bar of pressure. Assume the following tabulated standard values at $T = 298\text{ K}$:

| Species | $\Delta H_f^\circ$ (kJ/mol) | $S^\circ$ (J/mol$\cdot$K) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| $\text{N}_2(g)$ | $0$ | $191.6$ |
| $\text{H}_2(g)$ | $0$ | $130.7$ |
| $\text{NH}_3(g)$ | $-46.1$ | $192.5$ |

#### 1. Calculation of $\Delta H^\circ$ and $\Delta S^\circ$

First, we calculate the standard enthalpy change of the reaction, $\Delta H^\circ$, by taking the difference between the enthalpies of formation of the products and the reactants:

$$\Delta H^\circ = \sum \nu_i \Delta H_{f,i}^\circ = 2(-46.1) - [0 + 3(0)] = -92.2\text{ kJ}$$

Next, we calculate the standard entropy change, $\Delta S^\circ$:

$$\Delta S^\circ = \sum \nu_i S_i^\circ = 2(192.5) - [191.6 + 3(130.7)] = 385.0 - 583.7 = -198.7\text{ J/K}$$

Note that $\Delta S^\circ$ is negative, which is physically expected since four moles of gas are being converted into two moles of gas, leading to a decrease in the number of available microstates.

#### 2. Spontaneity at Room Temperature ($T = 298\text{ K}$)

We now apply the Gibbs free energy relation. We must ensure units are consistent, converting $\Delta S^\circ$ to kJ/K:

$$\Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T\Delta S^\circ$$
$$\Delta G^\circ = -92.2\text{ kJ} - (298\text{ K})(-0.1987\text{ kJ/K})$$
$$\Delta G^\circ = -92.2 + 59.2 = -33.0\text{ kJ}$$

Since $\Delta G^\circ < 0$, the synthesis of ammonia is spontaneous at $298\text{ K}$. At this temperature, the exothermic nature of the reaction ($\Delta H < 0$) outweighs the unfavorable decrease in entropy.

#### 3. Spontaneity at High Temperature ($T = 700\text{ K}$)

In industrial practice, the reaction is often carried out at much higher temperatures to increase the reaction rate (kinetics), despite the thermodynamic cost. Assuming $\Delta H^\circ$ and $\Delta S^\circ$ remain approximately constant over this range:

$$\Delta G^\circ = -92.2\text{ kJ} - (700\text{ K})(-0.1987\text{ kJ/K})$$
$$\Delta G^\circ = -92.2 + 139.1 = +46.9\text{ kJ}$$

At $700\text{ K}$, $\Delta G^\circ > 0$. The reaction is no longer spontaneous in the forward direction; instead, the reverse reaction (decomposition of ammonia) becomes spontaneous.

### Analysis of Temperature Dependence

The results above illustrate the "competition" between enthalpy and entropy. The term $\Delta H$ favors spontaneity (since it is negative), while the term $-T\Delta S$ opposes it (since $\Delta S$ is negative, $-T\Delta S$ is positive). As $T$ increases, the magnitude of the entropy term grows, eventually dominating the enthalpy term.

The "crossover" temperature $T_{eq}$, where the reaction reaches equilibrium ($\Delta G = 0$), is found by:

$$0 = \Delta H^\circ - T_{eq}\Delta S^\circ \implies T_{eq} = \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{\Delta S^\circ}$$
$$T_{eq} = \frac{-92.2\text{ kJ}}{-0.1987\text{ kJ/K}} \approx 464\text{ K}$$

Below $464\text{ K}$, the reaction is enthalpy-driven and spontaneous. Above $464\text{ K}$, the reaction is entropy-forbidden and non-spontaneous. A plot of $\Delta G$ versus $T$ would show a straight line with a positive slope (since $\Delta S < 0$), crossing the $T$-axis at $464\text{ K}$.

**Exercise: Predicting Spontaneity**
A reaction has $\Delta H^\circ = +20\text{ kJ}$ and $\Delta S^\circ = +150\text{ J/K}$. 
1. Is the reaction spontaneous at $298\text{ K}$?
2. At what temperature does the reaction become spontaneous?

**Solution:**
1. $\Delta G^\circ = 20\text{ kJ} - (298\text{ K})(0.150\text{ kJ/K}) = 20 - 44.7 = -24.7\text{ kJ}$. Yes, it is spontaneous.
2. Since $\Delta H > 0$ and $\Delta S > 0$, the reaction is spontaneous at high temperatures. The crossover occurs at $T = \Delta H/\Delta S = 20 / 0.150 \approx 133.3\text{ K}$. It is spontaneous for all $T > 133.3\text{ K}$.
