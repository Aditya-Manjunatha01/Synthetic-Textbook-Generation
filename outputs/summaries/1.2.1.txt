[SUMMARY: 1.2.1 — Work, Heat, and the Formal Statement of the First Law]

ESTABLISHED:
  — Definition of macroscopic work for a quasi-static process: work is the integral of pressure over volume change.
  — Definition of heat as energy transfer driven by temperature gradients.
  — The First Law of Thermodynamics for closed systems: the change in internal energy is the difference between heat added and work performed.
  — Mathematical distinction between exact differentials (state functions, path-independent) and inexact differentials (process variables, path-dependent).
  — The result that the difference between two inexact differentials ($\delta Q$ and $\delta W$) yields an exact differential ($dU$).

NOT YET DEVELOPED:
  — Application of the First Law to non-quasi-static processes.
  — Energy balance for open systems (variable mass flow).

NOTATION INTRODUCED:
  — $U$ : internal energy [J]
  — $W$ : work done by the system [J]
  — $\delta W$ : infinitesimal work [J]
  — $Q$ : heat added to the system [J]
  — $\delta Q$ : infinitesimal heat [J]
  — $P$ : pressure [Pa]
  — $V$ : volume [m³]
  — $A$ : piston area [m²]

PEDAGOGICAL APPROACH:
  — Full derivation of quasi-static work.
  — Physical intuition provided for heat vs. work and path dependence.
  — Mathematical rigor introduced via the distinction between exact and inexact differentials.
  — No worked examples or exercises included in this subsection.

KEY EQUATIONS:
  — $\delta W = P \, dV$
  — $W = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} P \, dV$
  — $\Delta U = Q - W$
  — $dU = \delta Q - \delta W$
