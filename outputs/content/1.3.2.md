### 1.3.2 Worked Example: Calculating Molar Heat Capacities for a Diatomic Gas with Rotational Freezing

In the previous section, we established that for an ideal gas, the molar heat capacity at constant volume is defined by the temperature dependence of the molar internal energy:
$$C_{V,m} = \left( \frac{\partial U_m}{\partial T} \right)_V$$
For a monatomic gas, the internal energy is purely translational, leading to the well-known result $C_{V,m} = \frac{3}{2}R$. However, for diatomic molecules, the situation is more complex because the molecule can store energy not only in its center-of-mass translation but also in its rotation and vibration.

#### The Concept of "Frozen" Degrees of Freedom

According to the classical Equipartition Theorem, every quadratic degree of freedom contributes $\frac{1}{2}RT$ to the molar internal energy. A linear diatomic molecule has three translational degrees of freedom and two rotational degrees of freedom (rotation about the two axes perpendicular to the internuclear bond). Classically, we would expect:
$$U_m = U_m^{trans} + U_m^{rot} = \frac{3}{2}RT + \frac{2}{2}RT = \frac{5}{2}RT$$
which implies $C_{V,m} = \frac{5}{2}R$.

However, quantum mechanics dictates that energy levels are discrete. For rotation, the energy levels are spaced such that a molecule can only be excited into a higher rotational state if the thermal energy $k_B T$ is comparable to the energy gap between the ground state and the first excited state. We define a characteristic rotational temperature, $\Theta_{rot}$, as:
$$\Theta_{rot} = \frac{\hbar^2}{2Ik_B}$$
where $I$ is the moment of inertia of the molecule. 

When $T \gg \Theta_{rot}$, the energy spacing is negligible compared to the thermal energy, and the rotational degrees of freedom behave classically (they are "active"). Conversely, when $T \ll \Theta_{rot}$, the molecules remain trapped in the lowest rotational energy state; the rotational degrees of freedom are said to be "frozen," and they contribute nothing to the heat capacity.

#### Worked Example: Nitrogen Gas ($\text{N}_2$)

Consider nitrogen gas, which has a characteristic rotational temperature of $\Theta_{rot} \approx 2.88\text{ K}$. We wish to determine the molar heat capacities $C_{V,m}$ and $C_{P,m}$ across a wide temperature range.

**1. The Internal Energy Model**
The total molar internal energy is the sum of the translational and rotational contributions:
$$U_m(T) = U_m^{trans} + U_m^{rot}(T)$$
The translational part remains classical at all temperatures of interest: $U_m^{trans} = \frac{3}{2}RT$. For the rotational part, we use the result derived from the rotational partition function in statistical mechanics. For $T \gg \Theta_{rot}$, $U_m^{rot} \approx RT$. For $T \to 0$, $U_m^{rot} \to 0$. A simplified expression for the rotational energy in the limit where $T$ is not extremely low is:
$$U_m^{rot}(T) \approx RT \left[ 1 - \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\Theta_{rot}}{T} \right)^2 \right]$$

**2. Deriving $C_{V,m}$**
We calculate the rotational contribution to the heat capacity, $C_{V,m}^{rot}$, by differentiating $U_m^{rot}$ with respect to $T$:
$$C_{V,m}^{rot} = \frac{d}{dT} \left( RT - \frac{R\Theta_{rot}^2}{2T} \right) = R + \frac{R\Theta_{rot}^2}{2T^2}$$
*(Note: This specific approximation is valid for $T > \Theta_{rot}$; at very low $T$, $C_{V,m}^{rot}$ drops exponentially to zero).*

The total molar heat capacity at constant volume is:
$$C_{V,m} = C_{V,m}^{trans} + C_{V,m}^{rot} = \frac{3}{2}R + R \left( 1 + \frac{\Theta_{rot}^2}{2T^2} \right)$$
In the high-temperature limit ($T \gg \Theta_{rot}$):
$$C_{V,m} \approx \frac{3}{2}R + R = \frac{5}{2}R$$
In the low-temperature limit ($T \ll \Theta_{rot}$), the rotational term vanishes:
$$C_{V,m} \approx \frac{3}{2}R$$

**3. Determining $C_{P,m}$**
Using Mayer's Relation established in Section 1.3.1, we find the molar heat capacity at constant pressure:
$$C_{P,m} = C_{V,m} + R$$
Substituting our result for $C_{V,m}$:
$$C_{P,m} = \left( \frac{3}{2}R + C_{V,m}^{rot}(T) \right) + R = \frac{5}{2}R + C_{V,m}^{rot}(T)$$
In the high-temperature limit: $C_{P,m} \approx \frac{7}{2}R$. In the low-temperature limit: $C_{P,m} \approx \frac{5}{2}R$.

#### Physical Interpretation and Visualization

If we were to plot $C_{V,m}$ as a function of temperature, we would observe a "step" behavior. At very low temperatures, the curve starts at a plateau of $\frac{3}{2}R$. As $T$ approaches $\Theta_{rot}$, the heat capacity rises sharply as the rotational degrees of freedom "unfreeze," eventually leveling off at a second plateau of $\frac{5}{2}R$.

It is important to note that at even higher temperatures (typically $T > 2000\text{ K}$ for $\text{N}_2$), the vibrational degrees of freedom also unfreeze. This would add another contribution to the internal energy, eventually driving $C_{V,m}$ toward $\frac{7}{2}R$. For the purposes of this example, we assume vibrational modes remain frozen.

**Summary of Results for $\text{N}_2$:**
| Temperature Regime | $C_{V,m}$ | $C_{P,m}$ | Active DOF |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $T \ll \Theta_{rot}$ | $\frac{3}{2}R$ | $\frac{5}{2}R$ | Translation only |
| $T \gg \Theta_{rot}$ | $\frac{5}{2}R$ | $\frac{7}{2}R$ | Translation + Rotation |
