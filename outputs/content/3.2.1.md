### 3.2.1 Thermodynamics of First-Order Phase Transitions and the Clausius-Clapeyron Equation

A phase transition occurs when a substance changes from one state of matter (e.g., liquid) to another (e.g., gas). From a thermodynamic perspective, we classify these transitions based on the behavior of the Gibbs free energy $G$ and its derivatives. A first-order phase transition is defined as a transition in which the first derivatives of the Gibbs free energy with respect to temperature and pressure are discontinuous at the transition point.

Recall that for a single-component system, the molar Gibbs free energy is equivalent to the chemical potential $\mu$. The first derivatives of $\mu(T, P)$ are the molar entropy $s$ and the molar volume $v$:
$$s = -\left( \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial T} \right)_P, \quad v = \left( \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial P} \right)_T$$
In a first-order transition, while $\mu$ remains continuous across the phase boundary to ensure equilibrium, $s$ and $v$ undergo abrupt changes. These discontinuities correspond to the latent heat of the transition and the change in density, respectively.

#### The Coexistence Curve and the Clapeyron Equation

Consider two phases, $\alpha$ and $\beta$, in thermodynamic equilibrium. As established in Subsection 3.1.3, the condition for coexistence is that their chemical potentials must be equal:
$$\mu_\alpha(P, T) = \mu_\beta(P, T)$$
This equality defines a curve in the $P-T$ plane known as the coexistence curve. If we move along this curve by an infinitesimal amount $(dT, dP)$, the two phases remain in equilibrium, meaning the change in chemical potential for each phase must be identical:
$$d\mu_\alpha = d\mu_\beta$$
Using the Gibbs-Duhem relation for a single component, $d\mu = -s dT + v dP$, we can substitute the expressions for each phase:
$$-s_\alpha dT + v_\alpha dP = -s_\beta dT + v_\beta dP$$
Rearranging this equation to group the entropy and volume terms, we obtain:
$$(s_\beta - s_\alpha) dT = (v_\beta - v_\alpha) dP$$
Defining the change in molar entropy as $\Delta s = s_\beta - s_\alpha$ and the change in molar volume as $\Delta v = v_\beta - v_\alpha$, we can solve for the slope of the coexistence curve:
$$\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta v}$$
For a reversible isothermal transition at temperature $T$, the change in molar entropy is related to the latent heat $L$ (the heat absorbed or released per mole) by $\Delta s = L/T$. Substituting this into the expression for the slope yields the Clapeyron Equation:
$$\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T \Delta v} \quad (3.2.1)$$
The Clapeyron equation is an exact result for any first-order transition, relating the slope of the phase boundary in the $P-T$ diagram to the macroscopic properties of the two phases.

#### The Clausius-Clapeyron Approximation

While the Clapeyron equation is general, it is often difficult to measure $\Delta v$ precisely, especially for liquid-vapor transitions. We can derive a more practical approximation, the Clausius-Clapeyron equation, by applying two simplifying assumptions to the case of vaporization (where $\alpha$ is liquid and $\beta$ is gas).

First, we assume that the molar volume of the gas $v_{gas}$ is much larger than the molar volume of the liquid $v_{liq}$, such that $\Delta v = v_{gas} - v_{liq} \approx v_{gas}$. Second, we assume that the vapor behaves as an ideal gas, allowing us to use the equation of state $v_{gas} = RT/P$. Substituting these into Equation (3.2.1), we find:
$$\frac{dP}{dT} \approx \frac{L}{T (RT/P)}$$
Rearranging this gives the Clausius-Clapeyron equation:
$$\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{P L}{RT^2} \quad (3.2.2)$$
This approximation is particularly powerful because it relates the variation of the equilibrium vapor pressure $P$ with temperature $T$ solely to the latent heat of vaporization and the universal gas constant $R$. In many practical cases, $L$ is assumed to be approximately constant over small temperature ranges, allowing for the integration of this equation to predict vapor pressures at different temperatures.
