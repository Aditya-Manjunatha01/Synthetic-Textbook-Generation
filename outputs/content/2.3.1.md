While the internal energy $U$ represents the total energy of a system, not all of this energy is available to perform useful work. In any process occurring at a finite temperature, a portion of the system's energy is inextricably linked to its entropy—the degree of disorder. The "free" energies, the Helmholtz free energy $F$ and the Gibbs free energy $G$, are designed to isolate the portion of the internal energy that can actually be converted into work, effectively subtracting the "unavailable" energy associated with the thermal state of the system.

### Helmholtz Free Energy and Maximum Work

To understand the physical meaning of $F$, consider a system undergoing an isothermal process at temperature $T$ while in contact with a thermal reservoir. The First Law of Thermodynamics states:
\begin{equation}
dU = \delta Q - \delta W
\end{equation}
where $\delta W$ is the work done by the system. From the Clausius inequality, we know that for any process, the heat exchange $\delta Q$ is constrained by the entropy change:
\begin{equation}
\delta Q \le T dS
\end{equation}
Substituting this inequality into the First Law, we obtain an upper bound for the work obtainable from the system:
\begin{equation}
dU \le T dS - \delta W \implies \delta W \le T dS - dU
\end{equation}
For an isothermal process ($dT = 0$), we recall the definition of the Helmholtz free energy from Section 2.2.1: $F = U - TS$. Its differential is $dF = dU - T dS - S dT$. Under isothermal conditions, this simplifies to $dF = dU - T dS$. Substituting this into the work inequality:
\begin{equation}
\delta W \le -dF
\end{equation}
Integrating this over a process from state 1 to state 2, we find that the total work $W$ is bounded by the change in Helmholtz free energy:
\begin{equation}
W \le -\Delta F
\end{equation}
The maximum work $W_{max}$ is achieved when the process is reversible ($\delta Q = T dS$), in which case the inequality becomes an equality:
\begin{equation}
W_{max} = -\Delta F
\end{equation}
Thus, $\Delta F$ represents the maximum amount of work a system can perform during an isothermal process.

### Gibbs Free Energy and Non-Expansion Work

In many practical scenarios, such as chemical reactions in an open beaker, the system is maintained at both constant temperature $T$ and constant pressure $P$. In such cases, the total work $W$ can be partitioned into the work required to push back the atmosphere (expansion work, $W_{PV} = \int P dV$) and any additional "useful" work, such as electrical or chemical work, denoted as $W_{non-PV}$.

The total work is given by $\delta W = P dV + \delta W_{non-PV}$. Substituting this into our isothermal work relation:
\begin{equation}
P dV + \delta W_{non-PV} \le -dF
\end{equation}
Rearranging for the non-expansion work:
\begin{equation}
\delta W_{non-PV} \le -dF - P dV
\end{equation}
At constant pressure, we use the relation $G = F + PV$. The differential of the Gibbs free energy is $dG = dF + P dV + V dP$. For a process at constant $P$ ($dP = 0$), we have $dG = dF + P dV$. Substituting this into the expression for non-expansion work:
\begin{equation}
\delta W_{non-PV} \le -dG
\end{equation}
Integrating this result, we find:
\begin{equation}
W_{non-PV} \le -\Delta G
\end{equation}
Consequently, the Gibbs free energy change $\Delta G$ represents the maximum non-expansion work obtainable from a system at constant temperature and pressure.

### Criteria for Spontaneity

The free energies also provide a powerful method for determining whether a process will occur spontaneously without needing to calculate the entropy change of the entire universe. The Second Law of Thermodynamics requires that for any spontaneous process, the total entropy change of the universe must be non-negative:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S_{univ} = \Delta S_{sys} + \Delta S_{surr} \ge 0
\end{equation}
If the system is in contact with a thermal reservoir at temperature $T$, any heat $\delta Q$ leaving the system enters the reservoir. Thus, $\Delta S_{surr} = -\Delta Q_{sys}/T$. For a process at constant $T$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S_{univ} = \Delta S_{sys} - \frac{\Delta Q_{sys}}{T} \ge 0 \implies T \Delta S_{sys} - \Delta Q_{sys} \ge 0
\end{equation}

**Case 1: Constant $T$ and $V$.**
At constant volume, no expansion work is done. If no other work is performed, the First Law gives $\Delta Q_{sys} = \Delta U$. The spontaneity condition becomes:
\begin{equation}
T \Delta S - \Delta U \ge 0 \implies \Delta U - T \Delta S \le 0
\end{equation}
Recognizing the definition of the Helmholtz free energy, we find that for $(T, V) = \text{const}$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta F \le 0
\end{equation}

**Case 2: Constant $T$ and $P$.**
At constant pressure, the heat exchange is equal to the change in enthalpy, $\Delta Q_{sys} = \Delta H$. The spontaneity condition becomes:
\begin{equation}
T \Delta S - \Delta H \ge 0 \implies \Delta H - T \Delta S \le 0
\end{equation}
Recognizing the definition of the Gibbs free energy, we find that for $(T, P) = \text{const}$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta G \le 0
\end{equation}

In summary, a process is spontaneous if it leads to a decrease in the appropriate free energy under the given constraints. When the free energy reaches a minimum ($\Delta F = 0$ or $\Delta G = 0$), the system has reached thermodynamic equilibrium.
