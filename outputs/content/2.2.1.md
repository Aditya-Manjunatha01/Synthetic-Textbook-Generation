### 2.2.1 Construction of Thermodynamic Potentials and the Legendre Transform

In the preceding sections, we established that for an isolated system, equilibrium is characterized by the maximization of entropy $S$ given a fixed internal energy $U$ and volume $V$. From a mathematical perspective, $U(S, V)$ serves as the fundamental relation, containing all possible thermodynamic information about the system. However, from an experimental perspective, the internal energy $U$ and entropy $S$ are often "unnatural" variables. While we can easily control the temperature $T$ of a system by placing it in a thermal reservoir, or the pressure $P$ by using a piston, we cannot directly "set" the entropy to a specific value using a laboratory dial.

To bridge the gap between the theoretical fundamental relation and experimental reality, we require a mathematical method to switch the independent variables of our state functions without losing any information. This is achieved through the Legendre transform.

#### The Fundamental Thermodynamic Relation

We begin by synthesizing the First Law of Thermodynamics with the definitions of entropy and quasi-static work. The First Law states that the change in internal energy is given by:
\begin{equation}
dU = \delta Q - \delta W
\end{equation}
For a reversible (quasi-static) process, we have the thermodynamic definition of entropy, $\delta Q = T dS$, and the expression for boundary work, $\delta W = P dV$. Substituting these into the First Law yields the fundamental thermodynamic relation:
\begin{equation}
dU = T dS - P dV
\end{equation}
This equation indicates that $U$ is a natural function of $S$ and $V$. The temperature and pressure are defined as the partial derivatives of the internal energy:
\begin{equation}
T = \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial S} \right)_{V}, \quad P = -\left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial V} \right)_{S}
\end{equation}

#### The Legendre Transform

Suppose we have a convex function $f(x)$. The information contained in $f(x)$ is completely captured by its slope $g = df/dx$. If we wish to describe the system using the slope $g$ as the independent variable instead of $x$, we cannot simply substitute $x(g)$ into $f(x)$, as this would result in a function that lacks the same derivative properties. Instead, we define a new function $h(g)$ via the Legendre transform $\mathcal{L}$:
\begin{equation}
h(g) = \mathcal{L}\{f(x)\} = f(x) - gx
\end{equation}
To verify that $h(g)$ preserves the information, we examine its differential:
\begin{equation}
dh = df - (g dx + x dg)
\end{equation}
Since $df = g dx$ by definition, the first two terms cancel, leaving:
\begin{equation}
dh = -x dg \implies x = -\frac{dh}{dg}
\end{equation}
Thus, the original variable $x$ is recovered as the negative slope of the new function $h(g)$. The Legendre transform allows us to switch from a variable (like $S$) to its conjugate derivative (like $T$) while maintaining a one-to-one mapping of the state of the system.

#### Construction of the Thermodynamic Potentials

We can now apply this operator to the internal energy $U(S, V)$ to construct potentials suited for different experimental constraints.

**1. Enthalpy ($H$): Switching $V \to P$**
To replace the volume $V$ with pressure $P$, we transform $U$ with respect to $V$. Since $P = -(\partial U / \partial V)_S$, the transform is:
\begin{equation}
H = U - (-P)V = U + PV
\end{equation}
Taking the differential of $H$:
\begin{equation}
dH = dU + P dV + V dP = (T dS - P dV) + P dV + V dP = T dS + V dP
\end{equation}
Enthalpy $H(S, P)$ is the natural potential for processes occurring at constant pressure.

**2. Helmholtz Free Energy ($F$): Switching $S \to T$**
To replace entropy $S$ with temperature $T$, we transform $U$ with respect to $S$. Since $T = (\partial U / \partial S)_V$:
\begin{equation}
F = U - TS
\end{equation}
The differential is:
\begin{equation}
dF = dU - T dS - S dT = (T dS - P dV) - T dS - S dT = -S dT - P dV
\end{equation}
The Helmholtz free energy $F(T, V)$ is the natural potential for systems held at constant temperature and volume.

**3. Gibbs Free Energy ($G$): Switching $S \to T$ and $V \to P$**
Finally, to replace both $S$ and $V$ with $T$ and $P$, we can apply a second transform to $F$ (switching $V \to P$) or to $H$ (switching $S \to T$):
\begin{equation}
G = F - (-P)V = F + PV = (U - TS) + PV = H - TS
\end{equation}
The differential is:
\begin{equation}
dG = dF + P dV + V dP = (-S dT - P dV) + P dV + V dP = -S dT + V dP
\end{equation}
The Gibbs free energy $G(T, P)$ is the most experimentally useful potential, as both $T$ and $P$ are typically controlled variables in a laboratory.

The relationship between these potentials can be summarized in the following map:
$U(S, V) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{L}_V} H(S, P) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{L}_S} G(T, P)$
$U(S, V) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{L}_S} F(T, V) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{L}_V} G(T, P)$

#### Worked Example: The Ideal Gas

Consider an ideal gas where the internal energy depends only on temperature, $U = C_V T$. From the ideal gas law $PV = nRT$, we can express $U$ in terms of $S$ and $V$. For a simplified case ($n=1$), the entropy is $S = C_V \ln T + R \ln V + S_0$. Solving for $T$:
\begin{equation}
T = \exp\left( \frac{S - R \ln V - S_0}{C_V} \right) \implies U(S, V) = C_V \exp\left( \frac{S - R \ln V - S_0}{C_V} \right)
\end{equation}
Working with $U(S, V)$ is algebraically cumbersome. Let us find the Helmholtz free energy $F(T, V)$ using the transform $F = U - TS$:
\begin{equation}
F = C_V T - T(C_V \ln T + R \ln V + S_0) = C_V T(1 - \ln T) - RT \ln V - TS_0
\end{equation}
Now, we can easily find the pressure of the gas by taking the derivative of $F$ with respect to its natural variable $V$:
\begin{equation}
P = -\left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial V} \right)_T = -\left( - \frac{RT}{V} \right) = \frac{RT}{V}
\end{equation}
This recovers the ideal gas law effortlessly, demonstrating that the Legendre transform allows us to move from a difficult-to-use energy representation to one that yields the equation of state through simple differentiation.
