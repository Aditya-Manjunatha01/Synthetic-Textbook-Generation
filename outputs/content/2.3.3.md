### 2.3.3 Exercise: Deriving the Gibbs-Helmholtz Equation and its Application to Temperature Dependence of Equilibrium Constants

In the preceding sections, we examined how the Gibbs free energy change $\Delta G$ determines the spontaneity of a process at a constant temperature $T$ and pressure $P$. However, in most practical chemical systems, temperature is a tunable parameter. To understand how the equilibrium position of a reaction shifts as we heat or cool the system, we require a mathematical relationship that describes the temperature dependence of the Gibbs free energy. This relationship is provided by the Gibbs-Helmholtz equation.

#### The Gibbs-Helmholtz Equation

We begin with the definition of the Gibbs free energy:
$$G = H - TS$$
To investigate the temperature dependence of $G$ at constant pressure, we consider the partial derivative of $G$ with respect to $T$. From the fundamental thermodynamic relations established in Section 2.2.2, we know that the entropy is given by:
$$S = -\left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial T} \right)_P$$
Substituting this expression for $S$ back into the definition of $G$, we obtain:
$$G = H + T \left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial T} \right)_P$$
To derive the Gibbs-Helmholtz equation, it is mathematically advantageous to consider the temperature dependence of the ratio $G/T$ rather than $G$ itself. We apply the quotient rule for differentiation with respect to $T$ at constant pressure:
$$\left( \frac{\partial(G/T)}{\partial T} \right)_P = \frac{1}{T} \left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial T} \right)_P - \frac{G}{T^2}$$
Using the relation $(\partial G / \partial T)_P = -S$, we substitute into the equation above:
$$\left( \frac{\partial(G/T)}{\partial T} \right)_P = \frac{-S}{T} - \frac{G}{T^2} = -\frac{TS + G}{T^2}$$
Recalling that $G = H - TS$, it follows that $TS + G = H$. Substituting this into our derivative yields the Gibbs-Helmholtz equation:
$$\left( \frac{\partial(G/T)}{\partial T} \right)_P = -\frac{H}{T^2}$$
Because the Gibbs free energy and enthalpy are state functions, this relationship applies equally to the *change* in these properties for a chemical process. For a reaction occurring at constant pressure, the change in Gibbs free energy $\Delta G$ and the change in enthalpy $\Delta H$ are related by:
$$\left( \frac{\partial(\Delta G/T)}{\partial T} \right)_P = -\frac{\Delta H}{T^2}$$

#### Application to the Equilibrium Constant: The van 't Hoff Equation

The Gibbs-Helmholtz equation is a powerful tool for linking the energetics of a reaction to its equilibrium state. We assume the standard result from chemical thermodynamics that the standard Gibbs free energy change $\Delta G^\circ$ is related to the equilibrium constant $K$ by:
$$\Delta G^\circ = -RT \ln K$$
where $R$ is the ideal gas constant. To find how $K$ varies with temperature, we substitute this relation into the Gibbs-Helmholtz equation:
$$\left( \frac{\partial(-RT \ln K / T)}{\partial T} \right)_P = -\frac{\Delta H^\circ}{T^2}$$
The $T$ terms inside the derivative cancel, simplifying the left-hand side:
$$\left( \frac{\partial(-R \ln K)}{\partial T} \right)_P = -\frac{\Delta H^\circ}{T^2}$$
Since $R$ is a constant, we can pull it out of the derivative:
$$-R \left( \frac{d \ln K}{dT} \right) = -\frac{\Delta H^\circ}{T^2}$$
Rearranging for the derivative of $\ln K$ gives the van 't Hoff equation:
$$\frac{d \ln K}{dT} = \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{RT^2}$$
This result demonstrates that the sign of the standard enthalpy change $\Delta H^\circ$ determines how the equilibrium constant responds to temperature. For an exothermic reaction ($\Delta H^\circ < 0$), increasing $T$ decreases $K$, shifting the equilibrium toward the reactants. For an endothermic reaction ($\Delta H^\circ > 0$), increasing $T$ increases $K$, shifting the equilibrium toward the products.

#### Worked Example: Calculating $K$ at a New Temperature

**Problem:** The equilibrium constant for a certain reaction is $K_1 = 1.5 \times 10^{-2}$ at $T_1 = 298\text{ K}$. The standard enthalpy change for the reaction is $\Delta H^\circ = 45\text{ kJ/mol}$. Assuming $\Delta H^\circ$ is constant over the temperature range, calculate the equilibrium constant $K_2$ at $T_2 = 350\text{ K}$.

**Solution:**
We start by integrating the van 't Hoff equation between the two temperatures:
$$\int_{K_1}^{K_2} d \ln K = \int_{T_1}^{T_2} \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{RT^2} dT$$
Assuming $\Delta H^\circ$ is independent of temperature, we integrate:
$$\ln \left( \frac{K_2}{K_1} \right) = \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{R} \int_{T_1}^{T_2} T^{-2} dT = \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{R} \left[ -\frac{1}{T} \right]_{T_1}^{T_2}$$
$$\ln \left( \frac{K_2}{K_1} \right) = \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{R} \left( \frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2} \right)$$
Plugging in the values ($R = 8.314\text{ J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$, $\Delta H^\circ = 45,000\text{ J/mol}$):
$$\ln \left( \frac{K_2}{1.5 \times 10^{-2}} \right) = \frac{45,000}{8.314} \left( \frac{1}{298} - \frac{1}{350} \right)$$
$$\ln \left( \frac{K_2}{1.5 \times 10^{-2}} \right) \approx 5412.5 \times (0.003356 - 0.002857) \approx 5412.5 \times 0.000499 \approx 2.70$$
Taking the exponential of both sides:
$$\frac{K_2}{1.5 \times 10^{-2}} = e^{2.70} \approx 14.88$$
$$K_2 \approx 14.88 \times 1.5 \times 10^{-2} \approx 0.223$$
**Interpretation:** Because the reaction is endothermic ($\Delta H^\circ > 0$), the equilibrium constant increased significantly as the temperature was raised from $298\text{ K}$ to $350\text{ K}$, as predicted by the van 't Hoff equation.

***

**Note on Simplifications:** In this derivation, we assumed that $\Delta H^\circ$ is constant over the temperature range. In reality, $\Delta H^\circ$ varies with temperature according to Kirchhoff's Law, which depends on the heat capacities of the reactants and products. This effect is typically small over narrow temperature ranges but becomes significant over larger intervals. The rigorous treatment of the temperature dependence of enthalpy is deferred to Chapter 3.
