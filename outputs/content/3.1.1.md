To describe systems where the amount of matter can change—either through chemical reactions or the exchange of particles with the surroundings—we must extend the laws of thermodynamics to open systems. In the previous chapters, we focused on closed systems where the number of particles was fixed. For a multicomponent system, the state is defined not only by the entropy $S$, volume $V$, and temperature $T$, but also by the number of moles $n_i$ of each species $i$ present in the system.

The fundamental relation for the internal energy $U$ of a simple closed system is given by the combined First and Second Laws as $dU = TdS - PdV$. For an open system, the internal energy must also depend on the composition. If we change the amount of species $i$ by an infinitesimal amount $dn_i$ while keeping the entropy, volume, and the amounts of all other species $n_{j \neq i}$ constant, the internal energy will change. This leads to the extended fundamental relation:

$$dU = TdS - PdV + \sum_{i} \mu_i dn_i$$

where $\mu_i$ is the chemical potential of species $i$. From this expression, we can formally define the chemical potential as the partial derivative of the internal energy with respect to the amount of substance $i$:

$$\mu_i = \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial n_i} \right)_{S, V, n_{j \neq i}} \quad (3.1)$$

Physically, $\mu_i$ represents the change in the internal energy of the system when one mole of species $i$ is added, provided the system remains at constant entropy and volume. However, in most experimental settings, it is far more practical to control temperature $T$ and pressure $P$ rather than entropy and volume. To shift our description to these variables, we employ a Legendre transform to define the Gibbs free energy $G$:

$$G = U + PV - TS$$

Taking the total differential of $G$, we have:

$$dG = dU + PdV + VdP - TdS - SdT$$

Substituting the extended fundamental relation for $dU$ into this expression, we find:

$$dG = (TdS - PdV + \sum_{i} \mu_i dn_i) + PdV + VdP - TdS - SdT$$

The $TdS$ and $PdV$ terms cancel, yielding the fundamental equation for the Gibbs free energy of a multicomponent system:

$$dG = -SdT + VdP + \sum_{i} \mu_i dn_i \quad (3.2)$$

From Equation 3.2, it follows that at constant temperature and pressure, the chemical potential of species $i$ is the rate of change of the total Gibbs free energy with respect to the amount of that species:

$$\mu_i = \left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial n_i} \right)_{T, P, n_{j \neq i}} \quad (3.3)$$

This identifies the chemical potential as the partial molar Gibbs free energy. Just as temperature governs the flow of heat and pressure governs the expansion of a gas, the chemical potential governs the flow of matter. 

The physical interpretation of $\mu_i$ is that of a "chemical pressure" or "escaping tendency." If a species $i$ exists in two different regions or phases with different chemical potentials, $\mu_i^{(1)}$ and $\mu_i^{(2)}$, there will be a spontaneous net flux of particles from the region of higher chemical potential to the region of lower chemical potential. This process continues until the chemical potentials are equalized, $\mu_i^{(1)} = \mu_i^{(2)}$, at which point the system reaches diffusive equilibrium. Consequently, the gradient of the chemical potential serves as the fundamental driving force for mass transport and the direction of chemical reactions.
