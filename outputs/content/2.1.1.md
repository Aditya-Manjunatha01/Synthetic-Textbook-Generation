### 2.1.1 Thermodynamic Definition of Entropy and the Clausius Inequality

The First Law of Thermodynamics establishes the conservation of energy, asserting that internal energy $U$ is a state function. However, the First Law is fundamentally blind to the direction of time. It permits any process—such as heat flowing spontaneously from a cold body to a hot body—provided the energy balance is maintained. Experience dictates that natural processes possess an inherent asymmetry; they occur spontaneously in one direction and not the reverse. To quantify this directionality, we must introduce a new state function: entropy.

#### The Definition of Entropy for Reversible Processes

Consider a system undergoing a quasi-static, reversible process. In such a process, the system remains in internal equilibrium at every infinitesimal step, and the process can be reversed without leaving any change in the universe. For such a path, the heat exchanged $\delta Q_{rev}$ is related to the temperature $T$ of the system.

We postulate the existence of a thermodynamic property $S$, termed entropy, defined by the relation:
\begin{equation}
dS = \frac{\delta Q_{rev}}{T}
\end{equation}
For this definition to be physically meaningful, $S$ must be a state function. This requires that the integral of $\delta Q_{rev}/T$ around any closed cyclic path be zero:
\begin{equation}
\oint \frac{\delta Q_{rev}}{T} = 0
\end{equation}
This result is a direct consequence of the Carnot cycle. Recall that for a reversible Carnot engine operating between a hot reservoir $T_h$ and a cold reservoir $T_c$, the efficiency $\eta$ is given by $\eta = 1 - T_c/T_h$. This implies that the ratio of heat exchanged to the temperature of the reservoir is the same for both the heat addition and heat rejection stages:
\begin{equation}
\frac{Q_h}{T_h} = \frac{Q_c}{T_c} \implies \frac{Q_h}{T_h} - \frac{Q_c}{T_c} = 0
\end{equation}
Since any reversible cycle can be decomposed into a series of infinitesimal Carnot cycles, it follows that the cyclic integral of $\delta Q_{rev}/T$ vanishes. Consequently, $dS$ is an exact differential, and the change in entropy $\Delta S$ between two states is path-independent:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S = S_2 - S_1 = \int_1^2 \frac{\delta Q_{rev}}{T}
\end{equation}

#### Transition to Irreversible Processes

In real-world scenarios, processes are rarely reversible. Irreversibility arises from dissipative effects such as friction, turbulence, or finite temperature gradients. Consider two paths connecting state 1 to state 2: a reversible path and an irreversible path.

While the change in entropy $\Delta S$ is the same for both paths (because $S$ is a state function), the heat exchanged differs. For a given change in internal energy $\Delta U$, the work performed by the system is maximized when the process is reversible. Conversely, for a process where heat is added, the "effective" heat transfer required to achieve a state change is minimized when the process is reversible. In an irreversible process, some of the energy that could have been used to perform work is instead dissipated as heat internally, effectively "creating" entropy within the system.

#### The Clausius Inequality

For any thermodynamic cycle, whether reversible or irreversible, the relationship between heat and temperature is governed by the Clausius Inequality:
\begin{equation}
\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} \leq 0
\end{equation}
In this expression, $T$ is the temperature of the reservoir with which the system is in contact. For a reversible cycle, as shown previously, the equality holds. For an irreversible cycle, the integral is strictly less than zero.

To understand this, consider a cycle consisting of an irreversible process from state 1 to state 2, and a reversible process returning the system from state 2 to state 1. The total change in entropy for the cycle must be zero:
\begin{equation}
\oint dS = \int_{1 \to 2} dS + \int_{2 \to 1} dS = 0
\end{equation}
Substituting the definition of $dS$ for the reversible return path:
\begin{equation}
\int_{1 \to 2} dS = - \int_{2 \to 1} \frac{\delta Q_{rev}}{T} = \int_{1 \to 2} \frac{\delta Q_{rev}}{T}
\end{equation}
For the irreversible leg ($1 \to 2$), the actual heat exchange $\delta Q_{irrev}$ is always less than the heat exchange required for a reversible transition between the same states ($\delta Q_{irrev} < \delta Q_{rev}$). Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S = \int_{1 \to 2} \frac{\delta Q_{rev}}{T} > \int_{1 \to 2} \frac{\delta Q_{irrev}}{T}
\end{equation}
Combining these results, we find that for any process:
\begin{equation}
dS \geq \frac{\delta Q}{T}
\end{equation}
Integrating this over a cycle yields $\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} \leq 0$. In integral form for a process between two states, this is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S \geq \int \frac{\delta Q}{T}
\end{equation}

Physically, this inequality implies that entropy can be transferred into a system via heat, but it can also be generated internally by irreversibilities. In an adiabatic process ($\delta Q = 0$), the inequality reduces to $dS \geq 0$, meaning the entropy of an isolated system can never decrease.
