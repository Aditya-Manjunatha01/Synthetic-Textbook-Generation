The study of thermodynamics begins with the act of definition. To analyze a physical process, we must first designate a specific region of the universe for our attention, which we term the **system**. Everything outside this region is referred to as the **surroundings**. The conceptual or physical interface that separates the system from its surroundings is the **boundary**. This boundary may be real, such as the walls of a piston-cylinder assembly, or imaginary, such as a mathematical surface enclosing a volume of fluid.

The nature of the boundary determines how the system interacts with its surroundings, allowing us to classify thermodynamic systems into three primary categories based on the exchange of matter and energy.

An **isolated system** is one whose boundary is completely impermeable to both matter and energy. In such a system, neither mass nor heat can cross the boundary, and no work can be performed on or by the surroundings. While perfectly isolated systems are idealizations, they serve as critical theoretical constructs for studying the evolution of a universe in equilibrium.

A **closed system** allows for the exchange of energy (in the form of heat or work) across the boundary, but prohibits the transfer of matter. The mass $m$ or the number of moles $n$ within a closed system remains constant over time, even as the system's internal energy $U$ may fluctuate due to interactions with the surroundings.

An **open system** is the most general case, where the boundary permits the exchange of both energy and matter. Such systems are common in engineering and biological contexts, such as a turbine or a living cell, where the continuous flow of mass is central to the system's function.

To describe the macroscopic condition of a system, we employ **state variables**. A state variable $X_i$ is a measurable property that depends only on the current condition of the system and not on the path the system took to reach that condition. Common examples include temperature $T$, pressure $P$, volume $V$, and internal energy $U$.

State variables are fundamentally divided into two classes based on how they scale with the size of the system. An **extensive variable** is one whose value is proportional to the amount of matter present. For instance, if the mass $m$ of a system is doubled while keeping other conditions constant, the volume $V$ and the internal energy $U$ will also double. Mathematically, if we scale the system size by a factor $\lambda$, an extensive variable $X_{ext}$ transforms as:
$$X_{ext} \rightarrow \lambda X_{ext}$$

In contrast, an **intensive variable** is independent of the system's size. Temperature $T$ and pressure $P$ are quintessential intensive variables; dividing a system in equilibrium into two equal halves does not halve the temperature or the pressure of the resulting subsystems. An intensive variable $X_{int}$ remains invariant under scaling:
$$X_{int} \rightarrow X_{int}$$

It is important to note that any extensive variable can be converted into an intensive one by dividing it by another extensive variable, typically the mass $m$ or the number of moles $n$. For example, the molar volume $V_m = V/n$ and the specific internal energy $u = U/m$ are intensive properties.

When a specific set of state variables is defined, the system is said to be in a particular **state**. We can visualize the state of a system as a single point in a multi-dimensional parameter space, where each axis represents a state variable. A change in the state of the system—caused by a change in $T, P, V,$ or $U$—corresponds to a trajectory through this space. If the system returns to its original state, the values of all state variables are restored, regardless of the process that occurred in the interim.
