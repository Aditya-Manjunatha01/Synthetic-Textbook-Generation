[SUMMARY: 2.3.1 — Thermodynamic Interpretation of Helmholtz and Gibbs Free Energies]

ESTABLISHED:
  — The physical interpretation of $F$ and $G$ as "free" energy available for work.
  — Derivation of the maximum work obtainable in an isothermal process using the First Law and Clausius inequality, resulting in $W \le -\Delta F$, with equality for reversible processes.
  — Derivation of the maximum non-expansion work for isothermal, isobaric processes, resulting in $W_{non-PV} \le -\Delta G$.
  — Spontaneity criteria derived from the Second Law ($\Delta S_{univ} \ge 0$):
    - At constant $T, V$: $\Delta F \le 0$.
    - At constant $T, P$: $\Delta G \le 0$.
  — Equilibrium is defined as the state where the relevant free energy is minimized ($\Delta F = 0$ or $\Delta G = 0$).

NOT YET DEVELOPED:
  — Application of spontaneity criteria to specific chemical reactions (deferred to 2.3.2).

NOTATION INTRODUCED:
  — $F$ : Helmholtz free energy [J]
  — $G$ : Gibbs free energy [J]
  — $W_{PV}$ : expansion work [J]
  — $W_{non-PV}$ : non-expansion work (e.g., electrical, chemical) [J]

PEDAGOGICAL APPROACH:
  — Full mathematical derivations starting from the First Law and Clausius inequality.
  — Physical intuition provided regarding "unavailable" energy and the partitioning of work.
  — No worked examples or exercises included in this subsection.

KEY EQUATIONS:
  — $W_{max} = -\Delta F$
  — $W_{non-PV} \le -\Delta G$
  — $\Delta S_{univ} = \Delta S_{sys} + \Delta S_{surr} \ge 0$
  — $(\Delta F)_{T,V} \le 0$
  — $(\Delta G)_{T,P} \le 0$
