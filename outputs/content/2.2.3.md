### 2.2.3 Worked Example: Using Maxwell Relations to Express the Internal Pressure in Terms of Measurable Quantities

A recurring challenge in thermodynamics is that the most fundamental state functions—namely the internal energy $U$ and the entropy $S$—are not directly measurable in a laboratory. We cannot "read" the absolute entropy of a sample from a gauge. In contrast, pressure $P$, volume $V$, and temperature $T$ are easily accessible via experimental measurement. 

To make thermodynamic theory useful for predicting the behavior of real substances, we must find ways to express derivatives of $U$ and $S$ in terms of $P, V,$ and $T$. A prime example of this is the "internal pressure," defined as the rate of change of internal energy with respect to volume at a constant temperature: $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T$. This quantity provides critical insight into the intermolecular forces within a substance; for an ideal gas, it is zero, but for real gases and liquids, it accounts for the energy associated with molecular attractions and repulsions.

***

**Problem:**
Using the fundamental thermodynamic relation and the Maxwell relations, derive an expression for the internal pressure $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T$ that depends only on the measurable quantities $P, V,$ and $T$. Then, verify that this result is consistent with Joule's Law for an ideal gas.

**Solution:**

We begin with the fundamental thermodynamic relation for a closed system, as established in Subsection 2.2.1:
$$dU = T dS - P dV$$
To find the internal pressure, we wish to determine how $U$ varies with $V$ while keeping $T$ constant. We divide the entire differential expression by $dV$, explicitly holding $T$ constant:
$$\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T = T \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T - P$$
At this stage, the expression is mathematically correct but physically impractical, as it contains the term $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T$. Entropy $S$ is not a directly measurable quantity, and its partial derivative with respect to volume is not readily available from experimental data.

To resolve this, we employ the Maxwell relations derived in Subsection 2.2.2. Specifically, we use the relation derived from the Helmholtz free energy $F(T, V)$, which links the entropy and pressure derivatives:
$$\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$$
Substituting this Maxwell relation into our expression for the internal pressure, we obtain:
$$\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T = T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V - P$$
This is the desired result. The internal pressure is now expressed entirely in terms of $T, P,$ and the temperature dependence of the pressure at constant volume—all of which can be determined from the equation of state of the substance.

**Validation for an Ideal Gas:**
For an ideal gas, the equation of state is given by:
$$P = \frac{nRT}{V}$$
To evaluate the term $\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$, we differentiate $P$ with respect to $T$ while holding $V$ constant:
$$\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{nR}{V}$$
Now, substitute this back into the derived expression for internal pressure:
$$\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T = T \left( \frac{nR}{V} \right) - P$$
Substituting the ideal gas law $P = \frac{nRT}{V}$ again:
$$\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T = \frac{nRT}{V} - \frac{nRT}{V} = 0$$
This result confirms that for an ideal gas, the internal energy is independent of volume at constant temperature, a result known as Joule's Law.

***

**Physical Insight:**
The fact that $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T = 0$ for an ideal gas reflects the underlying assumption that there are no intermolecular forces between the particles. In a real gas, however, $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T$ is generally non-zero. If the molecules attract one another, increasing the volume (increasing the average separation) requires work to overcome these attractions, thereby increasing the internal energy. The Maxwell relations allow us to quantify these microscopic interactions using only macroscopic, measurable properties.
