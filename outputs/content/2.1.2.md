### 2.1.2 Worked Example: Entropy Change in an Irreversible Free Expansion of an Ideal Gas

To illustrate the practical application of entropy as a state function, we consider a classic thought experiment known as the Joule expansion. This process highlights the fundamental distinction between the heat exchanged during a process and the change in entropy of the system.

#### Physical Setup
Consider a rigid, thermally insulated container divided into two equal compartments by a removable partition. One compartment contains $n$ moles of an ideal gas at an initial temperature $T_i$ and volume $V_i$, while the other compartment is a vacuum. The container is surrounded by an adiabatic wall, ensuring that no heat is exchanged with the environment ($\delta Q = 0$).

When the partition is suddenly removed, the gas undergoes a "free expansion," rushing to fill the entire volume $V_f$ of the container.

#### Analysis of the Irreversible Process
The free expansion is inherently irreversible. Because the gas expands into a vacuum, it does not push against an external pressure; consequently, no work is performed by the gas:
$$\delta W = 0$$
Since the container is thermally insulated, the process is adiabatic:
$$\delta Q = 0$$
Applying the First Law of Thermodynamics, we find the change in internal energy $\Delta U$:
$$\Delta U = \delta Q - \delta W = 0$$
For an ideal gas, the internal energy is a function of temperature only, $U = U(T)$. Therefore, a change $\Delta U = 0$ implies that the initial and final temperatures must be identical:
$$T_i = T_f = T$$
The process is thus isothermal, despite the absence of a heat reservoir.

#### Calculating Entropy Change via a Reversible Path
We wish to calculate the entropy change $\Delta S$ for this process. However, the definition of entropy change, $dS = \delta Q_{rev}/T$, requires a reversible path. Because entropy is a state function, the change $\Delta S$ depends only on the initial state $(T, V_i)$ and the final state $(T, V_f)$, and not on the path taken. We can therefore replace the actual irreversible expansion with a hypothetical reversible isothermal expansion between the same two states.

For a reversible isothermal process, the First Law states:
$$\delta Q_{rev} = dU + \delta W_{rev}$$
Since the temperature is constant, $dU = 0$ for the ideal gas. The reversible work done by the gas is given by the integral of $P dV$. Substituting the ideal gas law $P = nRT/V$, we have:
$$\delta Q_{rev} = P dV = \frac{nRT}{V} dV$$
We can now calculate the entropy change by integrating the definition of $dS$ from the initial volume $V_i$ to the final volume $V_f$:
$$\Delta S = \int_{V_i}^{V_f} \frac{\delta Q_{rev}}{T} = \int_{V_i}^{V_f} \frac{nRT}{V T} dV$$
The temperature $T$ cancels out, leaving a simple logarithmic integral:
$$\Delta S = nR \int_{V_i}^{V_f} \frac{dV}{V} = nR \ln\left(\frac{V_f}{V_i}\right)$$

#### Physical Interpretation
The result $\Delta S = nR \ln(V_f/V_i)$ is positive since $V_f > V_i$. This is a striking result: the entropy of the gas increases even though no heat entered the system ($\delta Q = 0$). 

This demonstrates that entropy is not simply a measure of heat transfer, but a measure of the state of the system. In an irreversible process, entropy is "produced" internally. The increase in entropy reflects the increased number of available microstates for the gas molecules as they occupy a larger volume, marking the transition from a more ordered (constrained) state to a more disordered (unconstrained) state.
