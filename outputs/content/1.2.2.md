### 1.2.2 Worked Example: Energy Balance in a Non-Quasistatic Compression Process

In the preceding section, we focused on quasistatic processes where the system remains in internal equilibrium at every instant. In such cases, the internal pressure $P_{int}$ is uniform throughout the volume and is approximately equal to the external pressure $P_{ext}$, allowing us to write the work done by the system as $\delta W = P dV$. However, many real-world processes occur rapidly, leading to non-quasistatic conditions. In these scenarios, pressure gradients develop within the fluid, and the internal pressure is no longer well-defined as a single scalar value. To calculate the work done, we must instead rely on the pressure exerted by the external agent.

Consider a cylinder containing $n$ moles of a monatomic ideal gas, initially at temperature $T_i$ and volume $V_i$. The cylinder is fitted with a piston. In a non-quasistatic process, a constant external pressure $P_{ext}$ is suddenly applied to the piston, compressing the gas to a final volume $V_f$. We assume the process occurs so rapidly that it is adiabatic ($Q = 0$), meaning there is no heat exchange with the surroundings.

To determine the energy balance, we first calculate the work done on the system. Since the external pressure $P_{ext}$ is constant throughout the compression, the work $W$ done *by* the gas is given by the integral of the external pressure over the change in volume:

$$W = \int_{V_i}^{V_f} P_{ext} dV = P_{ext}(V_f - V_i)$$

Because the gas is being compressed ($V_f < V_i$), the resulting value for $W$ is negative, indicating that work is being done *on* the system by the environment.

According to the First Law of Thermodynamics, the change in internal energy $\Delta U$ is:

$$\Delta U = Q - W$$

Given the adiabatic condition $Q = 0$, the energy balance simplifies to:

$$\Delta U = -P_{ext}(V_f - V_i) = P_{ext}(V_i - V_f)$$

For an ideal gas, the internal energy is a function of temperature only. For a monatomic gas, the molar heat capacity at constant volume is $C_V = \frac{3}{2}R$. The change in internal energy can thus be expressed as:

$$\Delta U = n C_V (T_f - T_i)$$

By equating the two expressions for $\Delta U$, we can solve for the final temperature $T_f$ of the gas:

$$n C_V (T_f - T_i) = P_{ext}(V_i - V_f)$$

Rearranging for $T_f$, we find:

$$T_f = T_i + \frac{P_{ext}(V_i - V_f)}{n C_V}$$

This result demonstrates that the final state of the system depends explicitly on the external agent's pressure $P_{ext}$. If we were to compare this to a quasistatic adiabatic compression between the same initial and final volumes, the work done would be $\int_{V_i}^{V_f} P_{int}(V) dV$. In a quasistatic process, $P_{int}$ increases as the volume decreases, whereas in this non-quasistatic case, the compressing force remains constant at $P_{ext}$. Consequently, the work done on the gas—and thus the final temperature $T_f$—is lower in the non-quasistatic process than in the reversible one.

On a $P-V$ diagram, a quasistatic process is represented by a smooth curve following the equation of state. In contrast, a non-quasistatic process cannot be represented by a single curve of equilibrium states, as the internal pressure $P_{int}$ is not uniform. The process is instead characterized by a "jump" from the initial equilibrium state $(P_i, V_i)$ to the final equilibrium state $(P_f, V_f)$, with the path between them being thermodynamically undefined.
