### 3.3.3 — Exercise: Analyzing the Behavior of Heat Capacities as $T \to 0$ to Ensure Finite Entropy

In the preceding sections, we established that the absolute entropy of a perfect crystal is given by the integral of the heat capacity divided by temperature:
\begin{equation}
S(T) = \int_0^T \frac{C_P(T')}{T'} dT'
\end{equation}
For this expression to be physically meaningful, the integral must converge at the lower limit $T' = 0$. If the integral were to diverge, the entropy of the substance would be infinite, which contradicts the Third Law of Thermodynamics. 

In this guided exercise, we will analyze the mathematical constraints placed on the temperature dependence of $C_P$ as $T \to 0$.

#### The Guided Exercise

**Problem Statement:** Consider a material whose low-temperature molar heat capacity follows a general power-law behavior:
\begin{equation}
C_P(T) = C_0 T^n
\end{equation}
where $C_0$ is a proportionality constant and $n$ is a dimensionless exponent. Determine the conditions on $n$ such that the absolute entropy $S(T)$ remains finite as $T \to 0$.

**Step 1: Substitution and Integration**
Substitute the power-law model for $C_P(T')$ into the expression for absolute entropy. 
\begin{equation}
S(T) = \int_0^T \frac{C_0 (T')^n}{T'} dT' = C_0 \int_0^T (T')^{n-1} dT'
\end{equation}
Perform the integration. Recall from calculus that for a power-law integral of the form $\int x^m dx$, the result is $\frac{x^{m+1}}{m+1}$ provided that $m \neq -1$.

**Step 2: Convergence Analysis**
Evaluate the resulting expression for $S(T)$ in the limit as $T \to 0$ for the following cases:
1.  **Case $n > 0$:** Does the expression approach a finite value?
2.  **Case $n = 0$:** If $n=0$, the integrand becomes $C_0/T'$. Evaluate the integral $\int_0^T \frac{C_0}{T'} dT'$. Does it converge?
3.  **Case $n < 0$:** If $n$ is negative, analyze the behavior of $(T')^{n-1}$ as $T' \to 0$. Does the integral converge?

**Step 3: Physical Conclusion**
Based on your analysis in Step 2, what is the necessary condition for the exponent $n$? What does this imply about the value of $C_P$ as the temperature approaches absolute zero?

---

#### Solution and Discussion

**Step 1:** Integrating the expression, we find:
\begin{equation}
S(T) = C_0 \left[ \frac{(T')^n}{n} \right]_0^T = \frac{C_0}{n} T^n
\end{equation}
This result holds as long as $n \neq 0$.

**Step 2:**
1.  For $n > 0$, as $T \to 0$, the term $T^n \to 0$. Thus, $S(T)$ vanishes smoothly, and the integral converges.
2.  For $n = 0$, the heat capacity is a constant $C_P = C_0$. The entropy integral becomes:
    \begin{equation}
    S(T) = C_0 \int_0^T \frac{1}{T'} dT' = C_0 \ln(T') \Big|_0^T
    \end{equation}
    Since $\ln(T') \to -\infty$ as $T' \to 0$, the integral diverges logarithmically.
3.  For $n < 0$, let $n = -|n|$. The integral is $\int_0^T (T')^{-|n|-1} dT'$. The antiderivative is $\frac{(T')^{-|n|}}{-|n|}$. As $T' \to 0$, the term $(T')^{-|n|} = 1/(T')^{|n|}$ diverges to infinity.

**Step 3:** For the entropy to remain finite and consistent with the Third Law ($S_0 = 0$), we must have $n > 0$. Physically, this means that the heat capacity $C_P$ cannot be a constant or increase as $T \to 0$; it must vanish as $T \to 0$.

#### Consistency with Standard Models
We can now verify that the common low-temperature approximations are consistent with this requirement:
*   **Debye Model:** In the Debye limit, $C_P \approx \beta T^3$. Here $n=3$. Since $3 > 0$, the entropy converges.
*   **Electronic Heat Capacity:** In metals, the electronic contribution is $C_{el} \approx \gamma T$. Here $n=1$. Since $1 > 0$, the entropy converges.
*   **Einstein Model:** The Einstein model predicts $C_V \propto \exp(-\Theta_E/T)$. An exponential decay vanishes faster than any power law $T^n$ as $T \to 0$, thus ensuring convergence.
