### 1.2.3 Exercise: Deriving the First Law for a System with Variable Mass Flow

Up to this point, we have treated thermodynamic systems as closed, meaning that while energy may cross the boundary in the form of heat or work, the amount of matter remains constant. However, many physical systems—such as a filling tank, a water pipe, or a jet engine—are open systems. In these cases, mass flows across the boundary, and with that mass comes an associated energy flux. To analyze such systems, we define a **control volume (CV)**: a fixed region in space through which mass and energy may flow.

#### Mass Balance
We begin by invoking the conservation of mass. For a control volume, the rate of change of the total mass $m$ within the volume must equal the net rate at which mass enters the system. If $\dot{m}_{in}$ and $\dot{m}_{out}$ represent the mass flow rates (measured in $\text{kg/s}$) at the various inlets and outlets, the mass balance is given by:

$$\frac{dm}{dt} = \sum \dot{m}_{in} - \sum \dot{m}_{out}$$

#### Energy Transport and Flow Work
When a differential mass $dm$ enters the control volume, it brings with it its internal energy $u$, its kinetic energy $\frac{1}{2}v^2$, and its potential energy $gz$ per unit mass. However, there is an additional energy component required to move this mass across the boundary.

Consider a fluid at pressure $P$ being pushed into the control volume. To move a differential mass $dm$ into the system, the surroundings must perform work. The volume occupied by this mass is $dV = v \, dm$, where $v$ is the specific volume. The work done by the surroundings to "push" this mass against the internal pressure $P$ is:

$$\delta W_{flow} = P \, dV = P v \, dm$$

This is termed **flow work**. It is a form of energy that is not stored as internal energy but is necessary to maintain the flow. Consequently, the total energy $\Phi$ associated with the mass crossing the boundary is the sum of the internal energy and the flow work:

$$\Phi = u + Pv$$

#### The Introduction of Enthalpy
The combination $u + Pv$ appears frequently in the analysis of open systems. To simplify the notation, we define a new thermodynamic property called **enthalpy** ($H$). For a total mass $m$, enthalpy is defined as $H = U + PV$. On a per-unit-mass basis, we define the **specific enthalpy** $h$ as:

$$h = u + Pv$$

Enthalpy effectively accounts for both the internal energy of the fluid and the work required to move that fluid into or out of the control volume.

#### The General Energy Balance
We now combine these elements to form the First Law for an open system. The rate of change of the total energy within the control volume is equal to the rate of heat transfer $\dot{Q}$, minus the rate of work done by the system $\dot{W}$ (such as shaft work from a turbine), plus the net energy flux carried by the mass flow.

The total specific energy $e$ of the fluid is the sum of its enthalpy, kinetic energy, and potential energy:
$$e = h + \frac{1}{2}v^2 + gz$$

Summing the contributions from all inlets and outlets, the energy balance in rate form is:

$$\frac{dU_{sys}}{dt} = \dot{Q} - \dot{W} + \sum \dot{m}_{in} \left( h + \frac{1}{2}v^2 + gz \right)_{in} - \sum \dot{m}_{out} \left( h + \frac{1}{2}v^2 + gz \right)_{out}$$

This equation represents the First Law of Thermodynamics for a control volume. It allows us to track energy changes in systems where mass is not conserved within the boundary, bridging the gap between the closed-system analysis of Section 1.2.1 and the study of steady-flow machinery.

**Worked Example: Energy Balance for a Steady-State Nozzle**

Consider a nozzle through which a fluid flows at a steady rate. A nozzle is designed to increase the velocity of a fluid by decreasing its pressure. We assume the following:
1. The process is steady-state, meaning the state of the fluid at any point in the nozzle does not change with time ($\frac{dU_{sys}}{dt} = 0$).
2. The nozzle is adiabatic ($\dot{Q} = 0$).
3. There is no shaft work ($\dot{W} = 0$).
4. Changes in potential energy are negligible ($gz \approx 0$).
5. There is one inlet and one outlet ($\dot{m}_{in} = \dot{m}_{out} = \dot{m}$).

**Problem:** Derive the relationship between the change in enthalpy and the change in velocity for the fluid.

**Solution:**
Starting with the general energy balance:
$$0 = 0 - 0 + \dot{m} \left( h_{in} + \frac{1}{2}v_{in}^2 \right) - \dot{m} \left( h_{out} + \frac{1}{2}v_{out}^2 \right)$$

Dividing by the mass flow rate $\dot{m}$ and rearranging the terms:
$$h_{in} + \frac{1}{2}v_{in}^2 = h_{out} + \frac{1}{2}v_{out}^2$$

Rearranging to solve for the change in enthalpy:
$$h_{in} - h_{out} = \frac{1}{2}(v_{out}^2 - v_{in}^2)$$

**Physical Interpretation:**
The result shows that the increase in the fluid's kinetic energy (the velocity increase) comes directly at the expense of its enthalpy. In a nozzle, the fluid's internal energy and flow work are converted into macroscopic kinetic energy.
