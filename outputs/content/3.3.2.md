The Third Law of Thermodynamics provides a unique advantage over the Second Law: it establishes an absolute reference point for entropy. While the Second Law allows us to calculate changes in entropy, $\Delta S$, the Third Law enables the determination of the absolute entropy, $S(T)$, of a substance. A primary application of this principle is the determination of the absolute entropy of crystalline solids through calorimetric measurements of heat capacity.

To find the absolute entropy of a substance at a temperature $T$, we begin with the fundamental thermodynamic identity for a constant-pressure process:
\begin{equation}
dS = \frac{C_P}{T} dT
\end{equation}
Integrating this expression from absolute zero to the temperature of interest, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
S(T) = S_0 + \int_{0}^{T} \frac{C_P(T')}{T'} dT'
\end{equation}
According to the Third Law, for a perfect crystalline substance, the entropy approaches zero as the temperature approaches absolute zero. Thus, we set the boundary condition $S_0 = 0$, and the absolute entropy is given by the integral of the heat capacity divided by temperature:
\begin{equation}
S(T) = \int_{0}^{T} \frac{C_P(T')}{T'} dT'
\end{equation}

In practice, calculating this integral presents an "experimental gap." It is physically impossible to measure $C_P$ at exactly $T = 0\text{ K}$, and measurements typically become unreliable or unavailable below a certain minimum temperature, $T_{low}$. To resolve this, the integration is split into two regions:
\begin{equation}
S(T) = \int_{0}^{T_{low}} \frac{C_P(T')}{T'} dT' + \int_{T_{low}}^{T} \frac{C_P(T')}{T'} dT'
\end{equation}
The second term is evaluated numerically using experimental data. The first term requires an extrapolation of $C_P$ to zero. For non-metallic crystalline solids at very low temperatures, the heat capacity follows the Debye $T^3$ law:
\begin{equation}
C_P \approx \beta T^3
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is a material-specific constant. Substituting this approximation into the low-temperature integral yields:
\begin{equation}
\int_{0}^{T_{low}} \frac{\beta T'^3}{T'} dT' = \int_{0}^{T_{low}} \beta T'^2 dT' = \frac{1}{3}\beta T_{low}^3
\end{equation}
The total absolute entropy is therefore the sum of this analytical extrapolation and the numerical integration of the experimental data from $T_{low}$ to $T$.

### Worked Example: Absolute Entropy of a Hypothetical Crystal

Consider a crystalline material where the low-temperature heat capacity coefficient is $\beta = 1.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K}^4)$. Experimental data for $C_P$ is available starting from $T_{low} = 10\text{ K}$. We wish to find the absolute entropy at $T = 30\text{ K}$.

**Data Table:**
| $T$ [K] | $C_P$ [J/(mol$\cdot$K)] | $C_P/T$ [J/(mol$\cdot$K$^2$)] |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 10 | 1.5 | 0.15 |
| 20 | 7.0 | 0.35 |
| 30 | 15.0 | 0.50 |

**Step 1: Low-temperature contribution ($0$ to $10\text{ K}$)**
Using the Debye approximation:
\begin{equation}
S_{low} = \frac{1}{3}\beta T_{low}^3 = \frac{1}{3}(1.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K}^4))(10\text{ K})^3 = 0.5 \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}
\end{equation}

**Step 2: Experimental contribution ($10$ to $30\text{ K}$)**
We approximate the integral $\int_{10}^{30} \frac{C_P}{T} dT$ using the trapezoidal rule:
\begin{equation}
S_{exp} \approx \sum \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{C_P(T_i)}{T_i} + \frac{C_P(T_{i+1})}{T_{i+1}} \right) (T_{i+1} - T_i)
\end{equation}
For the interval $10\text{ K}$ to $20\text{ K}$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{2}(0.15 + 0.35)(20 - 10) = 0.25 \times 10 = 2.5 \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}
\end{equation}
For the interval $20\text{ K}$ to $30\text{ K}$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{2}(0.35 + 0.50)(30 - 20) = 0.425 \times 10 = 4.25 \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}
\end{equation}
Thus, $S_{exp} = 2.5 + 4.25 = 6.75 \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}$.

**Step 3: Total Absolute Entropy**
\begin{equation}
S(30\text{ K}) = S_{low} + S_{exp} = 0.5 + 6.75 = 7.25 \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}
\end{equation}

Visually, this process corresponds to calculating the area under a curve of $C_P/T$ plotted against $T$. The region from $0$ to $T_{low}$ is represented by a parabolic curve ($\beta T^2$) that meets the experimental data points at $T_{low}$, ensuring a continuous transition from the theoretical zero-point to the measured regime.
