### 1.3.1 Internal Energy and Heat Capacities of Ideal Gases

From a microscopic perspective, the internal energy $U$ of a system is the sum of all microscopic forms of energy: the kinetic energy of the molecules (translational, rotational, and vibrational) and the potential energy arising from intermolecular forces. For an ideal gas, we assume the particles are point-like and non-interacting except during elastic collisions. Consequently, there are no intermolecular potential energy contributions. This suggests that the internal energy should depend solely on the temperature $T$, as $T$ is a direct measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles.

Mathematically, for a simple compressible system, the internal energy $U$ is a state function that can depend on any two independent state variables. We express it as a function of temperature and volume:
$$U = U(T, V)$$
The total differential of $U$ is then given by:
$$dU = \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial T} \right)_V dT + \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial V} \right)_T dV$$

#### Joule's Law for Ideal Gases

To determine whether the internal energy of an ideal gas depends on its volume, we consider a thought experiment known as the Joule expansion (or free expansion). Imagine two insulated bulbs connected by a stopcock; one bulb contains the ideal gas while the other is evacuated. When the stopcock is opened, the gas expands to fill both bulbs.

Because the container is insulated, the process is adiabatic ($\delta Q = 0$). Because the gas expands into a vacuum, it does not push against an external pressure, meaning no macroscopic work is performed ($\delta W = 0$). Applying the First Law of Thermodynamics, $dU = \delta Q - \delta W$, we find that:
$$dU = 0 \implies \Delta U = 0$$
In this process, the gas expands (increasing $V$) while the temperature remains constant ($T = \text{constant}$). Since $\Delta U = 0$ for a change in volume at constant temperature, it follows that:
$$\left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial V} \right)_T = 0$$
This result is known as Joule's Law for ideal gases. It implies that the internal energy of an ideal gas is independent of its volume and depends only on its temperature:
$$U = U(T)$$
For a system containing $n$ moles of gas, we define the molar internal energy $U_m = U/n$. Thus, for an ideal gas, $U_m$ is a function of temperature alone.

#### Molar Heat Capacities

The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a substance depends on the conditions under which the heating occurs. We define the molar heat capacity as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one mole of the substance by one Kelvin.

**Constant Volume Heat Capacity ($C_{V,m}$)**
Consider a process where the gas is heated while held at a constant volume ($dV = 0$). In this case, the work done $\delta W = P dV = 0$. The First Law simplifies to:
$$dU = \delta Q_V$$
We define the molar heat capacity at constant volume, $C_{V,m}$, as:
$$C_{V,m} = \frac{1}{n} \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial T} \right)_V$$
Since we have established that $U$ for an ideal gas depends only on $T$, the partial derivative becomes a total derivative, and we can write:
$$dU = n C_{V,m} dT \quad (1)$$

**Constant Pressure Heat Capacity ($C_{P,m}$)**
Now consider a process where the gas is heated while the pressure is held constant. In this case, the gas is allowed to expand to maintain the pressure. The heat added, $\delta Q_P$, is defined by the molar heat capacity at constant pressure, $C_{P,m}$:
$$C_{P,m} = \frac{1}{n} \left( \frac{\delta Q}{dT} \right)_P \implies \delta Q_P = n C_{P,m} dT \quad (2)$$

#### Mayer's Relation

There is a fundamental difference between $C_{V,m}$ and $C_{P,m}$ because, at constant pressure, a portion of the added heat is used to perform expansion work rather than solely increasing the internal energy. To find the relationship between them, we apply the First Law for a constant-pressure process:
$$\delta Q_P = dU + P dV$$
Substituting the expression for $dU$ from Equation (1):
$$\delta Q_P = n C_{V,m} dT + P dV \quad (3)$$
For an ideal gas, the Ideal Gas Law is $PV = nRT$. At constant pressure, the change in volume is:
$$P dV = nR dT$$
Substituting this into Equation (3):
$$\delta Q_P = n C_{V,m} dT + nR dT = n (C_{V,m} + R) dT$$
Comparing this result with Equation (2), we see that:
$$n C_{P,m} dT = n (C_{V,m} + R) dT$$
Dividing by $n dT$, we arrive at Mayer's Relation:
$$C_{P,m} - C_{V,m} = R$$
This relation demonstrates that $C_{P,m}$ is always greater than $C_{V,m}$ by the universal gas constant $R$, reflecting the additional energy required to perform work against the external pressure during isobaric heating.
