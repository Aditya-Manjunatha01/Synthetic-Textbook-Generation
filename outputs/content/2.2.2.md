The thermodynamic potentials—internal energy $U$, enthalpy $H$, Helmholtz free energy $F$, and Gibbs free energy $G$—are state functions. By definition, the differential of a state function is an exact differential, meaning its value depends only on the initial and final states of the system and not on the path taken between them. This mathematical property allows us to derive a set of powerful equalities known as the Maxwell relations.

From multivariable calculus, we recall Schwarz's theorem on the equality of mixed partial derivatives. If a function $z(x, y)$ has a differential of the form
$$dz = M(x, y)dx + N(x, y)dy$$
and if the second partial derivatives are continuous, then the differential $dz$ is exact if and only if
$$\left( \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right)_x = \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} \right)_y$$
In thermodynamics, the potentials are functions of their natural variables, and their differentials are expressed in terms of other state variables. By applying this condition to each potential, we can relate the derivatives of these variables to one another.

We begin with the internal energy $U(S, V)$. Its fundamental relation is given by
$$dU = TdS - PdV$$
Comparing this to the general form $dz = Mdx + Ndy$, we identify $M = T$, $N = -P$, $x = S$, and $y = V$. Applying the condition for an exact differential, we obtain
$$\left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial V} \right)_S = -\left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial S} \right)_V$$
This is the first Maxwell relation. It relates the change in temperature with respect to volume at constant entropy to the change in pressure with respect to entropy at constant volume.

Next, we consider the enthalpy $H(S, P)$. The differential for enthalpy is
$$dH = TdS + VdP$$
Here, the natural variables are $S$ and $P$. Identifying $M = T$ and $N = V$, the condition for an exact differential yields
$$\left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial P} \right)_S = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} \right)_P$$
Similarly, for the Helmholtz free energy $F(T, V)$, the differential is
$$dF = -SdT - PdV$$
Identifying $M = -S$ and $N = -P$ with natural variables $T$ and $V$, we find
$$\left( \frac{\partial (-S)}{\partial V} \right)_T = \left( \frac{\partial (-P)}{\partial T} \right)_V \implies \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial V} \right)_T = \left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial T} \right)_V$$
Finally, we apply this to the Gibbs free energy $G(T, P)$, which has the differential
$$dG = -SdT + VdP$$
With natural variables $T$ and $P$, and $M = -S$ and $N = V$, the exactness of $dG$ implies
$$\left( \frac{\partial (-S)}{\partial P} \right)_T = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial T} \right)_P \implies -\left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial P} \right)_T = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial T} \right)_P$$

The four Maxwell relations are summarized in the table below, mapped to the potential from which they originate.

| Potential | Differential | Maxwell Relation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Internal Energy $U(S, V)$ | $dU = TdS - PdV$ | $\left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial V} \right)_S = -\left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial S} \right)_V$ |
| Enthalpy $H(S, P)$ | $dH = TdS + VdP$ | $\left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial P} \right)_S = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} \right)_P$ |
| Helmholtz Free Energy $F(T, V)$ | $dF = -SdT - PdV$ | $\left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial V} \right)_T = \left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial T} \right)_V$ |
| Gibbs Free Energy $G(T, P)$ | $dG = -SdT + VdP$ | $-\left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial P} \right)_T = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial T} \right)_P$ |

To visualize these relationships, one can employ a mnemonic device known as the "Thermodynamic Square." By placing the variables $S, V, T, P$ at the corners of a square and the potentials at the center or along the edges, the Maxwell relations appear as opposing paths or symmetries within the square. This geometric interpretation highlights that the relations are not merely algebraic coincidences but are deeply rooted in the topological structure of the state space.
