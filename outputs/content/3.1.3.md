Thermodynamic equilibrium in a complex system is characterized by the absence of any macroscopic driving force that would cause the system to evolve over time. In a multicomponent, multiphase system, such driving forces can manifest as gradients in temperature, pressure, or chemical potential. For a system to be in complete equilibrium, it must simultaneously satisfy the conditions for thermal, mechanical, and chemical equilibrium.

Consider a closed system consisting of multiple phases, indexed by $\alpha, \beta, \dots$. Each phase $\alpha$ contains a set of chemical species $i$, with the amount of species $i$ in phase $\alpha$ denoted by $n_{i,\alpha}$. The total Gibbs free energy of the system, $G_{total}$, is the sum of the Gibbs free energies of the individual phases:

$$G_{total} = \sum_{\alpha} G_{\alpha}(T_{\alpha}, P_{\alpha}, \{n_{i,\alpha}\})$$

For a system maintained at constant overall temperature $T$ and pressure $P$, the condition for equilibrium is that the total Gibbs free energy be minimized. Consequently, any infinitesimal change in the state of the system at equilibrium must result in a total differential of zero:

$$dG_{total} = 0$$

To find the specific requirements for equilibrium, we expand the total differential of $G_{total}$ by considering the contributions from each phase $\alpha$:

$$dG_{total} = \sum_{\alpha} \left( \frac{\partial G_{\alpha}}{\partial T_{\alpha}} dT_{\alpha} + \frac{\partial G_{\alpha}}{\partial P_{\alpha}} dP_{\alpha} + \sum_{i} \frac{\partial G_{\alpha}}{\partial n_{i,\alpha}} dn_{i,\alpha} \right) = 0$$

Using the fundamental thermodynamic relation for the Gibbs free energy, $dG = -SdT + VdP + \sum \mu_i dn_i$, we can rewrite the expression as:

$$dG_{total} = \sum_{\alpha} \left( -S_{\alpha} dT_{\alpha} + V_{\alpha} dP_{\alpha} + \sum_{i} \mu_{i,\alpha} dn_{i,\alpha} \right) = 0$$

### Thermal and Mechanical Equilibrium

If the phases are allowed to exchange heat, any temperature difference $T_{\alpha} \neq T_{\beta}$ would drive a spontaneous heat flow to increase the total entropy of the universe, thereby decreasing $G_{total}$ at constant $T, P$. For the summation $\sum_{\alpha} -S_{\alpha} dT_{\alpha}$ to vanish for arbitrary heat exchanges, the temperature must be uniform across all phases:

$$T_{\alpha} = T_{\beta} = T \quad \text{for all } \alpha, \beta$$

Similarly, if the phases are separated by movable boundaries (such as a piston or a fluid interface), they can exchange volume. For the term $\sum_{\alpha} V_{\alpha} dP_{\alpha}$ to vanish under these conditions, the pressure must be equal in every phase:

$$P_{\alpha} = P_{\beta} = P \quad \text{for all } \alpha, \beta$$

### Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical equilibrium concerns the redistribution of species between phases. Consider the transfer of an infinitesimal amount of species $i$, $dn_i$, from phase $\alpha$ to phase $\beta$. Because the system is closed, the total amount of species $i$ is conserved, implying:

$$dn_{i,\alpha} = -dn_{i,\beta}$$

Substituting this constraint into the chemical potential term of the $dG_{total}$ expression for a two-phase system:

$$dG_{total, \text{chem}} = \mu_{i,\alpha} dn_{i,\alpha} + \mu_{i,\beta} dn_{i,\beta} = (\mu_{i,\alpha} - \mu_{i,\beta}) dn_{i,\alpha}$$

For the system to be at equilibrium, $dG_{total, \text{chem}}$ must be zero for any arbitrary transfer $dn_{i,\alpha}$. This requires:

$$\mu_{i,\alpha} = \mu_{i,\beta} \quad \text{for all species } i \text{ and all phases } \alpha, \beta$$

Physically, the chemical potential $\mu_{i,\alpha}$ represents the "escaping tendency" of species $i$ from phase $\alpha$. Equilibrium is reached when the escaping tendency of a component is identical in every phase it inhabits.

### Summary of Equilibrium Conditions

A multicomponent, multiphase system is in complete thermodynamic equilibrium if and only if the following three conditions are satisfied for all phases $\alpha$ and $\beta$, and all species $i$:

1. **Thermal Equilibrium:** $T_{\alpha} = T_{\beta}$
2. **Mechanical Equilibrium:** $P_{\alpha} = P_{\beta}$
3. **Chemical Equilibrium:** $\mu_{i,\alpha} = \mu_{i,\beta}$

**Worked Example: Liquid-Vapor Equilibrium of a Binary Mixture**

Consider a mixture of two volatile liquids, A and B, in equilibrium with their vapor. We wish to find the equilibrium composition of the vapor phase given the liquid composition.

**Problem:** A liquid solution contains mole fractions $x_A$ and $x_B$. Assuming the liquid behaves as an ideal solution and the vapor as an ideal gas, find the mole fraction of A in the vapor phase, $y_A$.

**Solution:**
At equilibrium, the chemical potential of species A in the liquid phase ($\mu_{A,liq}$) must equal its chemical potential in the vapor phase ($\mu_{A,vap}$):

$$\mu_{A,liq} = \mu_{A,vap}$$

Using the expressions for ideal solutions and ideal gases:
$$\mu_{A,liq}^* (T, P) + RT \ln x_A = \mu_A^\circ(T) + RT \ln \left( \frac{y_A P}{P^\circ} \right)$$

For the pure component A in equilibrium with its own vapor at temperature $T$ (where $x_A=1$ and $y_A=1$), the pressures are equal to the vapor pressure $P_A^*$:
$$\mu_{A,liq}^* (T, P) = \mu_A^\circ(T) + RT \ln \left( \frac{P_A^*}{P^\circ} \right)$$

Substituting this back into the equilibrium condition:
$$\mu_A^\circ(T) + RT \ln \left( \frac{P_A^*}{P^\circ} \right) + RT \ln x_A = \mu_A^\circ(T) + RT \ln \left( \frac{y_A P}{P^\circ} \right)$$

Canceling common terms and exponentiating both sides:
$$x_A P_A^* = y_A P$$

This is Raoult's Law. The mole fraction in the vapor phase is therefore:
$$y_A = \frac{x_A P_A^*}{P}$$

**Interpretation:** The equilibrium distribution of species between phases is governed by the balance of their chemical potentials. In this case, the "push" from the liquid (represented by $x_A P_A^*$) is exactly balanced by the "push" from the vapor (represented by $y_A P$).
