The theoretical utility of the Clausius-Clapeyron relation is best demonstrated through its application to real-world systems. A common example of high-pressure liquid-vapor transitions is found in industrial autoclaves or household pressure cookers. In these devices, the internal pressure is intentionally increased above atmospheric levels to raise the boiling point of water, allowing food or medical equipment to be heated to temperatures well above $100^\circ\text{C}$ while remaining in the liquid phase, thereby accelerating chemical reactions or sterilization processes.

To calculate the boiling point of water at a specific high pressure, we begin with the differential form of the Clausius-Clapeyron equation derived in the previous section:

$$\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{PL}{RT^2}$$

To find the temperature $T_2$ corresponding to a target pressure $P_2$, we separate the variables $P$ and $T$:

$$\frac{dP}{P} = \frac{L}{R T^2} dT$$

Integrating both sides from the initial state $(P_1, T_1)$—typically standard atmospheric pressure and the normal boiling point—to the final state $(P_2, T_2)$, we obtain:

$$\int_{P_1}^{P_2} \frac{dP}{P} = \int_{T_1}^{T_2} \frac{L}{R T^2} dT$$

Assuming the molar latent heat of vaporization $L$ remains constant over the temperature range of interest, we can pull the constants $L$ and $R$ out of the integral:

$$\ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = \frac{L}{R} \int_{T_1}^{T_2} T^{-2} dT = \frac{L}{R} \left[ -\frac{1}{T} \right]_{T_1}^{T_2}$$

This yields the integrated form of the Clausius-Clapeyron equation:

$$\ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = \frac{L}{R} \left( \frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2} \right) \quad (3.2.4)$$

To solve for the final boiling temperature $T_2$, we rearrange the expression algebraically:

$$\frac{1}{T_2} = \frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{R}{L} \ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)$$

Taking the reciprocal provides the direct formula for the boiling point:

$$T_2 = \left[ \frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{R}{L} \ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) \right]^{-1}$$

### Worked Example: Water at 10 atm

**Given:**
- Initial pressure: $P_1 = 1\text{ atm}$
- Initial boiling point: $T_1 = 373.15\text{ K}$ ($100^\circ\text{C}$)
- Target pressure: $P_2 = 10\text{ atm}$
- Molar latent heat of vaporization for water: $L \approx 40.65\text{ kJ/mol} = 40,650\text{ J/mol}$
- Universal gas constant: $R = 8.314\text{ J}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}$

**Substitution:**
Using the rearranged form of Equation (3.2.4):

$$\frac{1}{T_2} = \frac{1}{373.15} - \frac{8.314}{40,650} \ln\left(\frac{10}{1}\right)$$

$$\frac{1}{T_2} \approx 0.0026799 - (0.0002045 \times 2.3026)$$

$$\frac{1}{T_2} \approx 0.0026799 - 0.0004708 = 0.0022091\text{ K}^{-1}$$

**Result:**
$$T_2 \approx 452.67\text{ K} \approx 179.5^\circ\text{C}$$

The calculated boiling point of water at $10\text{ atm}$ is approximately $179.5^\circ\text{C}$.

### Analysis of Limitations

The actual boiling point of water at $10\text{ atm}$ is approximately $180^\circ\text{C}$. The small discrepancy between our result and the experimental value arises from the assumption that $L$ is constant. In reality, the latent heat of vaporization decreases as temperature increases, eventually vanishing at the critical point. Furthermore, at higher pressures, the assumption that the vapor behaves as an ideal gas becomes less accurate. These deviations are typically negligible for modest pressure increases but become significant as the system approaches the critical point, where non-ideal compressibility factors must be introduced.
