### 3.2.3 — Exercise: Deriving the Clapeyron Equation for the Solid-Liquid Transition of Water and the Resulting Slope of the Melting Curve

In most substances, the solid phase is denser than the liquid phase. Consequently, increasing the pressure on a substance typically raises its melting temperature, as the system is pushed toward the state with the smaller molar volume (the solid). Water is a notable exception to this rule. The observation that ice melts under pressure—a phenomenon critical to the movement of glaciers and the behavior of ice skates—suggests that the melting curve of water in the $P-T$ plane must possess a negative slope.

In this exercise, we will use the general Clapeyron equation to derive the mathematical basis for this anomalous behavior.

#### The Guided Derivation

**Step 1: Specialization of the Clapeyron Equation**
Recall from Section 3.2.1 that the slope of the coexistence curve for any first-order phase transition is given by the Clapeyron equation:
$$\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T \Delta v}$$
For the transition from solid (ice) to liquid (water), known as fusion, we specialize the terms to denote the molar latent heat of fusion $L_{\text{fus}}$ and the change in molar volume $\Delta v_{\text{fus}}$. The slope of the melting curve at a given melting temperature $T_m$ and pressure $P_m$ is therefore:
$$\left( \frac{dP}{dT} \right)_{\text{fus}} = \frac{L_{\text{fus}}}{T_m \Delta v_{\text{fus}}}$$

**Step 2: Analysis of the Molar Volume Change**
The change in molar volume during fusion is defined as the difference between the molar volume of the liquid phase and the molar volume of the solid phase:
$$\Delta v_{\text{fus}} = v_{\text{water}} - v_{\text{ice}}$$
Given the molar mass of water $M_{\text{H}_2\text{O}}$ and the mass densities of the two phases, $\rho_{\text{water}}$ and $\rho_{\text{ice}}$, we can express the molar volumes as $v = M/\rho$. Substituting these into the expression for $\Delta v_{\text{fus}}$ yields:
$$\Delta v_{\text{fus}} = \frac{M_{\text{H}_2\text{O}}}{\rho_{\text{water}}} - \frac{M_{\text{H}_2\text{O}}}{\rho_{\text{ice}}} = M_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \left( \frac{1}{\rho_{\text{water}}} - \frac{1}{\rho_{\text{ice}}} \right)$$

**Step 3: Determining the Sign of the Slope**
To determine the sign of $dP/dT$, we examine the physical properties of water:
1. **Latent Heat:** Fusion is an endothermic process; heat must be added to break the crystalline lattice of ice. Thus, $L_{\text{fus}} > 0$.
2. **Temperature:** The absolute temperature $T_m$ is always positive.
3. **Density:** It is an established physical property of water that ice is less dense than liquid water ($\rho_{\text{ice}} < \rho_{\text{water}}$).

Looking at the expression for $\Delta v_{\text{fus}}$, since $\rho_{\text{ice}} < \rho_{\text{water}}$, it follows that $1/\rho_{\text{ice}} > 1/\rho_{\text{water}}$. This implies:
$$\Delta v_{\text{fus}} < 0$$

**Step 4: Conclusion on the Melting Curve**
Substituting these signs back into the Clapeyron equation:
$$\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{(+)}{(+)(-)} < 0$$
We have proven that for the solid-liquid transition of water, the slope of the coexistence curve is negative.

#### Physical Interpretation and the Phase Diagram

The result $\frac{dP}{dT} < 0$ means that as the pressure $P$ increases, the equilibrium melting temperature $T_m$ must decrease to maintain the phase coexistence. Physically, because liquid water is denser than ice, the application of pressure favors the liquid state, allowing ice to melt at temperatures below $0^\circ\text{C}$ (at 1 atm).

This is clearly visible in the $P-T$ phase diagram of water. While the liquid-vapor coexistence line (the boiling curve) always has a positive slope—since the vapor is always significantly less dense than the liquid—the solid-liquid line (the melting curve) leans to the left. This negative slope is the thermodynamic signature of water's anomalous expansion upon freezing.
