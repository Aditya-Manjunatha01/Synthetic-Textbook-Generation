### 3.1.2 Worked Example: Chemical Potential of an Ideal Gas and Ideal Solution

To move from the abstract definition of the chemical potential as a partial molar Gibbs free energy to its practical application, we examine two fundamental models: the ideal gas mixture and the ideal solution. These models provide the baseline for understanding how concentration and pressure drive the transport of matter.

Before proceeding, we define the mole fraction $x_i$ of species $i$ in a mixture of $N$ components as the ratio of the number of moles of $i$ to the total number of moles in the system:
$$x_i = \frac{n_i}{\sum_{j=1}^N n_j}$$
By definition, the sum of all mole fractions in a mixture is unity, $\sum x_i = 1$.

#### Worked Example 1: The Ideal Gas Mixture

Consider a pure ideal gas. From the fundamental relation for the Gibbs free energy $G$, we know that at constant temperature, the pressure dependence is given by $(\partial G / \partial P)_T = V$. For one mole of an ideal gas, $V_m = RT/P$. Integrating this from a reference pressure $P^\circ$ (typically 1 bar) to an arbitrary pressure $P$ yields:
$$G(T, P) = G^\circ(T) + RT \ln \left( \frac{P}{P^\circ} \right)$$
where $G^\circ(T)$ is the molar Gibbs free energy of the pure gas at the reference pressure. Since for a pure substance the chemical potential $\mu$ is simply the molar Gibbs free energy, we have:
$$\mu(T, P) = \mu^\circ(T) + RT \ln \left( \frac{P}{P^\circ} \right) \quad (3.14)$$
Now, consider a mixture of ideal gases. In an ideal mixture, the particles are non-interacting, and the total Gibbs free energy is the sum of the contributions of each species. For a component $i$ in a mixture, the relevant pressure is its partial pressure $P_i$, which according to Dalton's Law is $P_i = x_i P$.

Substituting the partial pressure into the expression for the chemical potential, we obtain the chemical potential for species $i$ in an ideal gas mixture:
$$\mu_i^{id} = \mu_i^\circ(T) + RT \ln \left( \frac{P_i}{P^\circ} \right) = \mu_i^\circ(T) + RT \ln \left( \frac{x_i P}{P^\circ} \right) \quad (3.15)$$
This result demonstrates that the chemical potential of a gas increases logarithmically with both its partial pressure and its mole fraction. Physically, this reflects the fact that increasing the concentration of a species increases its "escaping tendency" from the phase.

#### Worked Example 2: The Ideal Solution

While the gas phase is governed by pressure, the liquid phase is typically described by composition. We define an **ideal solution** as a mixture in which the interaction energies between unlike molecules are identical to those between like molecules. Mathematically, this implies that the enthalpy of mixing is zero ($\Delta H_{mix} = 0$) and the volume change upon mixing is zero ($\Delta V_{mix} = 0$).

For such a system, the only contribution to the Gibbs free energy of mixing comes from the entropy of mixing. For a mixture of indistinguishable particles of different species, the standard result for the entropy of mixing is:
$$\Delta S_{mix} = -R \sum_i n_i \ln x_i$$
Given that $\Delta G_{mix} = \Delta H_{mix} - T\Delta S_{mix}$, the Gibbs free energy of mixing for an ideal solution is:
$$\Delta G_{mix} = RT \sum_i n_i \ln x_i$$
The chemical potential of species $i$ in the mixture is the partial molar Gibbs free energy:
$$\mu_i = \left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial n_i} \right)_{T, P, n_{j \neq i}}$$
The total Gibbs free energy of the solution is $G = \sum n_i \mu_i^*(T, P) + \Delta G_{mix}$, where $\mu_i^*$ is the chemical potential of the pure liquid $i$ at the same temperature and pressure. Performing the differentiation:
$$\mu_i = \mu_i^*(T, P) + RT \ln x_i \quad (3.16)$$
This is the governing equation for ideal solutions. It shows that the chemical potential of a solute decreases as it becomes more dilute ($x_i \to 0$). 

To visualize this dependence, imagine a plot of $\mu_i$ versus $\ln x_i$. The relationship is linear with a slope of $RT$. As the mole fraction decreases, the chemical potential drops sharply, which explains why solutes spontaneously dissolve in pure solvents—the chemical potential of the solute in the pure state ($\mu_i^*$) is higher than its chemical potential in the solution.

This result is the thermodynamic basis for Raoult's Law. If the liquid is in equilibrium with its vapor, then $\mu_{i, liquid} = \mu_{i, gas}$. Using equations (3.15) and (3.16), and assuming the vapor is an ideal gas, one can show that the partial pressure of the component $i$ above the solution is $P_i = x_i P_i^*$, where $P_i^*$ is the vapor pressure of the pure liquid.

***

**Note on Non-Ideality:** The results derived here assume that the molecules do not deviate from "ideal" behavior. In real systems, intermolecular forces lead to non-zero enthalpies of mixing and deviations from the ideal gas law. To account for this, the pressure $P_i$ in Eq. (3.15) is replaced by **fugacity** ($f_i$), and the mole fraction $x_i$ in Eq. (3.16) is replaced by **activity** ($a_i = \gamma_i x_i$), where $\gamma_i$ is the activity coefficient. These extensions are deferred to the discussion of real solutions.
