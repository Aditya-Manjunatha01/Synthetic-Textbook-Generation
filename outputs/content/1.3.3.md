### 1.3.3 Internal Energy and Heat Capacities of Real Gases via the Joule-Thomson Effect

In the preceding sections, we established that for an ideal gas, the internal energy $U$ is a function of temperature alone, meaning $(\partial U/\partial V)_T = 0$. This result arises because ideal gas particles are point-like and non-interacting. However, for real gases, the presence of intermolecular forces—both the long-range attraction and short-range repulsion discussed in Section 1.1.3—implies that the internal energy must depend on the average distance between molecules, and thus on the volume.

To investigate this dependence experimentally, we utilize the Joule-Thomson expansion. Unlike the Joule expansion (free expansion into a vacuum), the Joule-Thomson process involves a steady-state flow of gas.

#### The Joule-Thomson Process

Consider an apparatus consisting of a high-pressure reservoir connected to a low-pressure chamber via a porous plug. The plug is designed with pores small enough to ensure that the gas flows through it slowly, maintaining a state of local equilibrium, but large enough to prevent the flow from being completely blocked. The entire apparatus is thermally insulated from the surroundings.

As the gas passes through the plug, it expands from a high pressure $P_1$ to a lower pressure $P_2$. Because the process is adiabatic ($\delta Q = 0$) and occurs at a steady state, we apply the First Law for an open system. For a steady-state flow through a control volume where no external work is performed other than the flow work, the enthalpy $H$ remains constant across the expansion:
$$ H_1 = H_2 $$
Such a process is termed an *isenthalpic expansion*. The change in temperature resulting from this pressure drop is the core of the Joule-Thomson effect.

#### The Joule-Thomson Coefficient

We define the Joule-Thomson coefficient, $\mu_{JT}$, as the rate of change of temperature with respect to pressure during an isenthalpic process:
$$ \mu_{JT} = \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial P} \right)_H $$
To express $\mu_{JT}$ in terms of measurable thermodynamic quantities, we treat enthalpy $H$ as a function of temperature $T$ and pressure $P$, $H = H(T, P)$. The total differential is:
$$ dH = \left( \frac{\partial H}{\partial T} \right)_P dT + \left( \frac{\partial H}{\partial P} \right)_T dP $$
Recalling the definition of the molar heat capacity at constant pressure, $C_{P,m} = \frac{1}{n} (\partial H / \partial T)_P$, and considering one mole of gas, we have:
$$ dH_m = C_{P,m} dT + \left( \frac{\partial H_m}{\partial P} \right)_T dP $$
For an isenthalpic process, $dH_m = 0$. Solving for the temperature gradient, we obtain:
$$ \mu_{JT} = \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial P} \right)_H = -\frac{1}{C_{P,m}} \left( \frac{\partial H_m}{\partial P} \right)_T $$

#### Relation to Internal Pressure

To understand the physical origin of $\mu_{JT}$, we relate the enthalpy derivative to the internal energy. By the definition of enthalpy, $H_m = U_m + PV_m$. Differentiating with respect to pressure at constant temperature:
$$ \left( \frac{\partial H_m}{\partial P} \right)_T = \left( \frac{\partial U_m}{\partial P} \right)_T + V_m + P \left( \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial P} \right)_T $$
Using the chain rule, we can write $(\partial U_m / \partial P)_T = (\partial U_m / \partial V_m)_T (\partial V_m / \partial P)_T$. Substituting this into the expression:
$$ \left( \frac{\partial H_m}{\partial P} \right)_T = V_m + \left[ \left( \frac{\partial U_m}{\partial V_m} \right)_T + P \right] \left( \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial P} \right)_T $$
The term $(\partial U_m / \partial V_m)_T$ is known as the *internal pressure* of the real gas. It represents the work done against intermolecular forces during an isothermal expansion. For an ideal gas, the internal pressure is zero; for a real gas, it is non-zero and reflects the molecular interactions described in Section 1.1.3.

#### Analysis via the Van der Waals Equation

We can now apply the Van der Waals equation of state to find a concrete expression for $\mu_{JT}$:
$$ \left( P + \frac{a}{V_m^2} \right)(V_m - b) = RT $$
For a gas where the pressure is not excessively high, we can approximate $V_m \approx RT/P$. The internal pressure for a Van der Waals gas is derived from the cohesive pressure term:
$$ \left( \frac{\partial U_m}{\partial V_m} \right)_T = -\frac{a}{V_m^2} $$
Substituting this and the approximation for $V_m$ into our expression for $(\partial H_m / \partial P)_T$:
$$ \left( \frac{\partial H_m}{\partial P} \right)_T \approx \frac{RT}{P} + \left[ -\frac{a}{(RT/P)^2} + P \right] \left( -\frac{RT}{P^2} \right) = \frac{RT}{P} + \frac{a}{RT} - \frac{RT}{P} = \frac{a}{RT} $$
(Note: In a more rigorous treatment, the $b$ parameter also contributes, but the attraction term $a$ dominates the temperature dependence). Thus, the Joule-Thomson coefficient for a Van der Waals gas is approximately:
$$ \mu_{JT} \approx \frac{1}{C_{P,m}} \left( \frac{2a}{RT} - \frac{b}{V_m} \right) \approx \frac{1}{C_{P,m}} \left( \frac{2a}{RT} - \frac{bP}{RT} \right) $$
From this, we see that $\mu_{JT}$ can be positive, negative, or zero. When $\mu_{JT} = 0$, the gas undergoes no temperature change upon expansion. The temperature at which this occurs is the *inversion temperature*, $T_{inv}$:
$$ T_{inv} \approx \frac{2a}{R b} \quad (\text{approximately}) $$
If $T < T_{inv}$, then $\mu_{JT} > 0$, and the gas cools upon expansion ($\partial T/\partial P > 0$ implies $T$ decreases as $P$ decreases). If $T > T_{inv}$, then $\mu_{JT} < 0$, and the gas heats up.

#### Comparison with Ideal Gases

For an ideal gas, $a = 0$ and $b = 0$. Consequently:
$$ \left( \frac{\partial U_m}{\partial V_m} \right)_T = 0 \implies \left( \frac{\partial H_m}{\partial P} \right)_T = V_m + P\left( \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial P} \right)_T = \frac{RT}{P} + P\left( -\frac{RT}{P^2} \right) = 0 $$
Thus, $\mu_{JT} = 0$ for all temperatures and pressures for an ideal gas. The Joule-Thomson effect is therefore a direct macroscopic signature of intermolecular forces.

**Worked Example: Cooling vs. Heating in Real Gases**

**Problem:** At room temperature ($298\text{ K}$), Nitrogen ($\text{N}_2$) has an inversion temperature of approximately $621\text{ K}$, while Helium ($\text{He}$) has an inversion temperature of approximately $40\text{ K}$. Predict whether each gas will cool or heat upon expansion through a porous plug at $298\text{ K}$.

**Solution:**
1. For Nitrogen: $T = 298\text{ K} < T_{inv} = 621\text{ K}$. Since the operating temperature is below the inversion temperature, $\mu_{JT} > 0$. A decrease in pressure ($dP < 0$) results in a decrease in temperature ($dT < 0$). Nitrogen cools.
2. For Helium: $T = 298\text{ K} > T_{inv} = 40\text{ K}$. Since the operating temperature is above the inversion temperature, $\mu_{JT} < 0$. A decrease in pressure ($dP < 0$) results in an increase in temperature ($dT > 0$). Helium heats up.

**Physical Interpretation:** In Nitrogen, attractive forces dominate at $298\text{ K}$; work must be done to pull molecules apart during expansion, which is sourced from the kinetic energy, lowering the temperature. In Helium, the extremely weak attraction means the repulsive "excluded volume" effects dominate even at room temperature, leading to heating upon expansion.
