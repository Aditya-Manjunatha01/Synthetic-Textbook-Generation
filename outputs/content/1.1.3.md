### 1.1.3 Equations of State from Ideal Gas to Van der Waals and Virial Expansions

The state of a pure substance is defined by the relationship between its intensive variables. As established in the previous subsection, for a single-phase system, the state space is restricted by an equation of state (EOS), which allows us to express one variable, such as pressure $P$, as a function of the others: $P = P(V_m, T)$. The simplest such relationship is the ideal gas law, which serves as the baseline for understanding all real gas behavior.

#### The Ideal Gas Limit
The ideal gas law assumes a collection of non-interacting point particles. In this limit, the molecules occupy no physical volume and exert no forces on one another except during instantaneous elastic collisions. Under these assumptions, the relationship between pressure, molar volume $V_m$, and temperature $T$ is given by:

\begin{equation}
PV_m = RT
\end{equation}

where $R$ is the universal gas constant. While this model is highly accurate at low pressures and high temperatures, it fails when the density of the gas increases, as the physical dimensions of the molecules and the intermolecular forces can no longer be neglected.

#### The Van der Waals Equation
To account for the deviations from ideality, Johannes Diderik van der Waals introduced two primary corrections to the ideal gas law: one for the finite size of the molecules and one for the attractive forces between them.

**1. The Excluded Volume Correction**
In a real gas, molecules are not mathematical points but finite spheres. Consequently, the volume available for any single molecule to move is not the total molar volume $V_m$, but rather $V_m$ minus the volume occupied by the other molecules. This "excluded volume" is parameterized by the constant $b$, which is proportional to the volume of the molecules themselves. The volume term is thus modified:
$$V_m \to (V_m - b)$$

**2. The Cohesion Pressure Correction**
Real molecules experience long-range attractive forces (Van der Waals forces). A molecule in the bulk of the gas is pulled equally in all directions, but a molecule approaching the container wall experiences a net inward pull from the molecules behind it. This reduces the frequency and force of collisions with the wall, thereby lowering the observed pressure $P$ compared to an ideal gas. 

The magnitude of this effect depends on the density of the particles (the number of molecules per unit volume) and the density of the particles they are interacting with. Since both factors are proportional to $1/V_m$, the reduction in pressure is proportional to $(1/V_m)^2$. We introduce the attraction parameter $a$ to quantify this effect, modifying the pressure term:
$$P \to \left( P + \frac{a}{V_m^2} \right)$$

Combining these two corrections yields the Van der Waals equation of state:

\begin{equation}
\left( P + \frac{a}{V_m^2} \right)(V_m - b) = RT
\end{equation}

Here, $a$ (with units $\text{Pa}\cdot\text{m}^6/\text{mol}^2$) represents the strength of the intermolecular attraction, and $b$ (with units $\text{m}^3/\text{mol}$) represents the excluded volume.

#### The Compressibility Factor
To quantify the deviation of a real gas from ideal behavior across different models, we define the compressibility factor $Z$:

\begin{equation}
Z = \frac{PV_m}{RT}
\end{equation}

For an ideal gas, $Z = 1$ for all $P$ and $T$. If $Z < 1$, the attractive forces dominate, making the gas more compressible than an ideal gas. If $Z > 1$, the excluded volume effects dominate, making the gas less compressible.

#### The Virial Expansion
While the Van der Waals equation provides a useful physical heuristic, it is an approximation. A more rigorous approach is to treat the deviation from ideality as a power series in terms of density (or $1/V_m$). This is known as the Virial expansion:

\begin{equation}
Z = 1 + \frac{B(T)}{V_m} + \frac{C(T)}{V_m^2} + \dots
\end{equation}

In this expansion, $B(T)$ is the second virial coefficient, $C(T)$ is the third, and so on. Physically, these coefficients represent contributions from multi-particle interactions: $B(T)$ accounts for interactions between pairs of molecules, $C(T)$ accounts for triplets, and higher-order terms account for increasingly complex clusters. At the limit of $V_m \to \infty$ (zero pressure), $Z \to 1$, recovering the ideal gas law.

**Worked Example: Calculating the Compressibility Factor**

*Problem:* Consider a real gas at $T = 300\text{ K}$ and $P = 10\text{ MPa}$. The Van der Waals parameters are $a = 0.135\text{ Pa}\cdot\text{m}^6/\text{mol}^2$ and $b = 3.9 \times 10^{-5}\text{ m}^3/\text{mol}$. Calculate the compressibility factor $Z$ and determine if the gas is more or less compressible than an ideal gas.

*Solution:*
First, we find the molar volume $V_m$. For high pressures, we can approximate $V_m$ using the ideal gas law to get a starting point:
$$V_{m, \text{ideal}} = \frac{RT}{P} = \frac{8.314 \times 300}{10^7} \approx 2.5 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{mol}$$

Now, we use the Van der Waals equation to find a more accurate $V_m$. Rearranging the EOS:
$$P = \frac{RT}{V_m - b} - \frac{a}{V_m^2}$$
Substituting the known values and solving for $V_m$ (typically via iteration or a cubic solver), we find $V_m \approx 2.1 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{mol}$.

Now, calculate $Z$:
$$Z = \frac{PV_m}{RT} = \frac{(10^7\text{ Pa})(2.1 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{mol})}{(8.314\text{ J/mol}\cdot\text{K})(300\text{ K})} \approx \frac{2100}{2494.2} \approx 0.842$$

*Interpretation:* Since $Z \approx 0.842 < 1$, the gas is more compressible than an ideal gas at this state. This indicates that the attractive forces between the molecules are the dominant correction to the ideal behavior.
