### 3.3.1 — The Nernst Heat Theorem and the Statement of the Third Law of Thermodynamics

The Second Law of Thermodynamics provides a powerful framework for calculating changes in entropy, $\Delta S$, but it leaves the absolute value of entropy undefined. Because entropy is defined via the integral of $\delta Q_{rev}/T$, any constant can be added to the result without violating the laws of thermodynamics. However, as experimental physics pushed toward the regime of absolute zero, it became evident that the behavior of matter at extremely low temperatures imposes a fundamental constraint on the absolute scale of entropy.

#### The Nernst Heat Theorem

At the turn of the 20th century, Walther Nernst observed that for any isothermal process involving condensed phases (solids or liquids), the entropy change $\Delta S$ vanishes as the temperature approaches absolute zero. This empirical observation, known as the Nernst Heat Theorem, can be stated as:

$$\lim_{T \to 0} \Delta S = 0$$

Consider a chemical reaction or a phase transition occurring at a constant temperature $T$. The change in entropy between the initial state (reactants) and the final state (products) is $\Delta S = S_{final} - S_{initial}$. Nernst's theorem asserts that as $T \to 0$, the difference between the entropies of any two equilibrium states of a condensed system becomes negligible. Physically, this implies that the "disorder" or the number of available microstates becomes independent of the specific nature of the substance as it reaches the ground state.

#### The Planck Statement of the Third Law

Max Planck extended Nernst's theorem by proposing a more fundamental statement regarding the absolute value of entropy. While Nernst focused on the *difference* in entropy, Planck postulated that the entropy of a pure, perfect crystalline substance approaches a universal constant as the temperature approaches absolute zero.

The Third Law of Thermodynamics is thus formally stated as:
As the temperature of a perfect crystal approaches absolute zero, the entropy approaches a constant value $S_0$.

$$ \lim_{T \to 0} S = S_0 $$

By convention, for a perfect crystal—where there is only one unique ground state (non-degenerate)—we set this constant to zero:
$$ S_0 = 0 $$

This convention provides the "zero point" necessary to define an absolute entropy scale. It allows us to calculate the absolute entropy of a substance at any temperature $T$ by integrating the heat capacity from absolute zero:
$$ S(T) = \int_0^T \frac{C_P(T')}{T'} dT' $$

#### Implications for Heat Capacities

The requirement that entropy remains finite and converges to a constant as $T \to 0$ has profound implications for the thermal properties of matter. Consider the relationship between entropy and the molar heat capacity at constant pressure, $C_P$:
$$ dS = \frac{C_P}{T} dT $$

To find the change in entropy from $T=0$ to a finite temperature $T$, we evaluate the integral:
$$ S(T) - S_0 = \int_0^T \frac{C_P(T')}{T'} dT' $$

For the entropy $S(T)$ to be a well-defined, finite value as $T \to 0$, the integral on the right-hand side must converge. If $C_P$ were to remain constant or approach a non-zero constant as $T \to 0$, the integral would behave like $\ln(T)$, which diverges logarithmically as $T \to 0$.

Therefore, for the Third Law to hold, the heat capacity must vanish as the temperature approaches absolute zero:
$$ \lim_{T \to 0} C_P = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{T \to 0} C_V = 0 $$

This result is a cornerstone of low-temperature physics. It indicates that as a system approaches the ground state, its ability to store thermal energy decreases, eventually vanishing at absolute zero.

#### Conceptual Summary

If one were to plot the entropy $S$ of various pure substances against temperature $T$, the curves would start at different values at room temperature, reflecting the different degrees of structural disorder. However, as $T$ decreases, these curves would converge, all meeting at the origin $(0,0)$ for perfect crystals. This convergence illustrates the "unattainability" of absolute zero; as $C_P \to 0$, the amount of heat that must be removed to lower the temperature further becomes infinitesimally small, yet the process of removing that heat becomes infinitely difficult.
