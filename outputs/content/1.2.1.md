### 1.2.1 Work, Heat, and the Formal Statement of the First Law

To understand how a thermodynamic system evolves from one equilibrium state to another, we must distinguish between the energy a system *possesses* and the energy that is *transferred* across its boundaries. As established in Section 1.1.1, the internal energy $U$ is a state function; it depends only on the current state of the system (defined by variables such as $P, V,$ and $T$) and not on the history of how the system reached that state. However, the mechanisms by which $U$ changes—specifically work and heat—are fundamentally different in nature.

#### Mechanical Work in Thermodynamics

In the context of thermodynamics, we primarily concern ourselves with macroscopic work. Consider a closed system consisting of a gas enclosed in a cylinder with a frictionless piston of area $A$. If the gas expands, it exerts a force $F$ on the piston. For a process that occurs quasi-statically—meaning the system remains infinitesimally close to equilibrium at every instant—the pressure $P$ is uniform throughout the volume. The force exerted by the gas is thus $F = PA$.

If the piston moves by an infinitesimal distance $dx$, the infinitesimal work $\delta W$ done by the system on its surroundings is:
$$\delta W = F \, dx = P A \, dx$$
Since the change in volume is $dV = A \, dx$, we can express the differential work as:
$$\delta W = P \, dV$$
For a finite change in state from an initial volume $V_1$ to a final volume $V_2$, the total work $W$ is the integral of this expression along the specific path taken in the state space:
$$W = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} P \, dV$$
It is critical to note that $W$ is not a property of the system. Because $P$ is generally a function of both $V$ and $T$, the value of the integral depends entirely on the path taken in the $P-V$ plane. For example, if a gas expands isothermally (constant $T$) versus adiabatically (no heat exchange), the pressure $P$ will vary differently as $V$ increases, resulting in different total work values even if the initial and final volumes are identical.

#### Heat and Path Dependence

While work is energy transfer mediated by a macroscopic force, heat $Q$ is energy transfer mediated by a temperature gradient. Heat flows from a region of higher temperature to one of lower temperature. Like work, heat is "energy in transit." It is not "contained" within a system; rather, it is a process variable.

The path dependence of work implies a corresponding path dependence for heat. If we transition a system from state $A$ to state $B$, the work $W$ performed depends on the path. Since the total energy of the universe is conserved, the amount of heat $Q$ exchanged must also vary with the path to ensure that the change in the system's internal energy $\Delta U$ remains constant for the same two endpoints.

#### The First Law of Thermodynamics

The First Law of Thermodynamics is a statement of the conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems. For a closed system, the change in internal energy $\Delta U$ is equal to the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system:
$$\Delta U = Q - W$$
Here, we adopt the convention that $Q > 0$ when heat is added to the system and $W > 0$ when the system performs work on its surroundings.

To express this in differential form, we consider an infinitesimal change in the state of the system. The change in internal energy is written as $dU$, while the infinitesimal transfers of heat and work are written as $\delta Q$ and $\delta W$:
$$dU = \delta Q - \delta W$$
The distinction between the notation $dU$ and $\delta Q$ or $\delta W$ is mathematically profound and central to the rigor of thermodynamics.

#### Exact and Inexact Differentials

A state function, such as $U(T, V)$, possesses an **exact differential**. This means that the integral of $dU$ depends only on the endpoints of the process:
$$\int_{A}^{B} dU = U(B) - U(A) = \Delta U$$
Mathematically, if $U = U(x, y)$, then $dU = \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x}\right)_y dx + \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\right)_x dy$, and the mixed partial derivatives are equal.

In contrast, $\delta Q$ and $\delta W$ are **inexact differentials**. They do not represent the difference between two values of a state function, but rather the accumulation of a quantity along a path. The integral of an inexact differential is not denoted by a difference in state variables, but simply as the total quantity exchanged:
$$\int_{A}^{B} \delta W = W_{A \to B} \quad \text{and} \quad \int_{A}^{B} \delta Q = Q_{A \to B}$$
The First Law thus reveals a remarkable symmetry: the difference between two inexact differentials ($\delta Q - \delta W$) results in an exact differential ($dU$). While heat and work are individually path-dependent, their combination is a state function.
